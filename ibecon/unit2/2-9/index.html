2.9

Market Failure — Public Goods

Non-rival, non-excludable goods, the free rider problem, and government responses.

You should be able to
  • Define a public good and explain its two key characteristics
  • Explain the free rider problem and why it leads to market failure
  • Explain why governments provide public goods directly or contract them out
  • Distinguish public goods from private goods and common pool resources
Note

This section is pending teacher review.

What is a Public Good?

A public good has two defining characteristics:

Non-rivalrous
One person's consumption does not reduce the amount available to others. Many people can consume the good simultaneously without diminishing it.
Non-excludable
It is impossible or prohibitively expensive to prevent people from consuming the good once it is provided — even those who have not paid.

Examples: national defence, street lighting, public fireworks displays, lighthouses, clean air.

Exam tip

Many students confuse "public good" with "goods provided by the government." This is wrong. A public good is defined by non-rivalry and non-excludability, not by who provides it. Governments sometimes provide private goods (e.g., healthcare), and some public goods could theoretically be provided privately.

Comparing Good Types

Good typeRivalrous?Excludable?Example
Private goodYesYesA sandwich, a car
Public goodNoNoNational defence, street lighting
Common pool resourceYesNoOcean fisheries, groundwater
Club good (artificially scarce)NoYesToll road, cable TV

The Free Rider Problem

Because public goods are non-excludable, people can benefit from them without paying. This creates the free rider problem:

  • Rational individuals have an incentive to let others pay for the good and consume it for free
  • If everyone free rides, no one pays — and the good is not provided at all (or is underprovided)
  • Private firms cannot charge for the good and make a profit → the market fails to provide it

This is why public goods represent a form of market failure — the free market produces zero (or too little) of them, even though they may generate large social benefits.

Government Responses

Direct government provision

The government produces and provides the public good itself, funded through taxation. Examples: national defence, public parks, street lighting.

Contracting out to the private sector

The government funds the public good but contracts a private firm to produce it. The government pays the firm directly, so the free rider problem is bypassed. Examples: government-funded construction of public buildings, privately managed prisons.

Key points to remember
  • Public good: non-rival + non-excludable
  • Free rider: benefits without paying; rational but leads to underprovision
  • Market fails to provide public goods → government must step in
  • Government options: direct provision or contracting out
  • Do not confuse "public good" with "government-provided good"

Practice Questions
1. Explain why a private firm would not profitably provide national defence, using the concepts of non-rivalry and non-excludability.
Show answer
National defence is non-rival — protecting one citizen does not reduce protection for others — and non-excludable — once defence is provided, it is impossible to exclude any citizen from its benefits, even those who refuse to pay. A private firm cannot charge individuals for the service because free riders would simply consume it without paying. Since the firm cannot generate revenue, it has no profit motive to provide defence. The market therefore fails to provide it, requiring government provision financed by compulsory taxation.
2. Distinguish between a public good and a common pool resource. Use examples.
Show answer
Both are non-excludable, but they differ in rivalry. A public good (e.g., street lighting) is also non-rival — many people can use it simultaneously without reducing its availability. A common pool resource (e.g., ocean fisheries) is rivalrous — one person's use reduces what is available for others. This is why common pool resources are prone to the tragedy of the commons (overuse leading to depletion), whereas public goods are prone to the free rider problem (underuse of the willingness to pay, leading to underprovision).
3. Is a toll road a public good? Explain your answer.
Show answer
No. A toll road is not a pure public good. It is excludable — drivers who do not pay can be physically prevented from using it at toll booths. Whether it is rivalrous depends on congestion: an uncongested toll road is non-rival (one driver does not reduce capacity for others), but a congested toll road becomes rivalrous. In economic terminology, a toll road is a club good (non-rival but excludable) in uncongested conditions, or a private good when congested.
