2.5

Elasticities of Demand

Price elasticity of demand (PED) and income elasticity of demand (YED) — definitions, calculations, determinants, and significance.

You should be able to
  • Define and calculate PED; interpret its value
  • Explain the determinants of PED
  • Explain the relationship between PED and total revenue
  • Explain PED along a straight-line demand curve HL
  • Define and calculate YED; interpret its sign and magnitude
  • Explain the significance of YED for firms and the economy HL

Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

Price Elasticity of Demand
PED = % change in quantity demanded ÷ % change in price

PED is always negative (inverse relationship between P and Qd), but is often expressed as an absolute value.

Interpreting PED

Value (|PED|)DescriptionMeaning
|PED| > 1Relatively elastic%ΔQd > %ΔP — consumers respond strongly to price changes
|PED| < 1Relatively inelastic%ΔQd < %ΔP — consumers respond weakly to price changes
|PED| = 1Unitary elastic%ΔQd = %ΔP — proportional response
|PED| = 0Perfectly inelasticQuantity demanded does not change regardless of price (vertical curve)
|PED| = ∞Perfectly elasticAny price increase causes quantity demanded to fall to zero (horizontal curve)

Determinants of PED

  • Number and closeness of substitutes — more and closer substitutes → more elastic (easier to switch)
  • Degree of necessity — necessities (e.g., insulin) are inelastic; luxuries are more elastic
  • Proportion of income spent — goods that take a large share of income tend to be more elastic (consumers are more price-sensitive)
  • Time period — demand is more elastic in the long run (consumers have more time to adjust habits and find substitutes)
Key point

Primary commodities (e.g., wheat, oil) tend to have lower PED than manufactured products. This is because primary commodities have fewer close substitutes and are often necessities in production, while manufactured goods (e.g., branded clothing) face more competition. HL

PED and Total Revenue

PEDPrice risesPrice falls
Elastic (|PED| > 1)TR falls (Qd falls proportionally more)TR rises (Qd rises proportionally more)
Inelastic (|PED| < 1)TR rises (Qd falls proportionally less)TR falls (Qd rises proportionally less)
Unitary (|PED| = 1)TR unchangedTR unchanged
HL only — PED along a straight-line demand curve

Along a straight-line downward-sloping demand curve, PED varies:

  • At the top of the curve (high price, low quantity): demand is elastic (|PED| > 1)
  • At the midpoint: |PED| = 1 (unitary elastic)
  • At the bottom (low price, high quantity): demand is inelastic (|PED| < 1)

This means the same demand curve can be elastic or inelastic depending on where you are on it.

Income Elasticity of Demand (YED)

Income Elasticity of Demand
YED = % change in quantity demanded ÷ % change in income

Interpreting YED

Sign/valueType of good
YED > 0 (positive)Normal good — demand rises as income rises
YED < 0 (negative)Inferior good — demand falls as income rises
0 < YED < 1Normal necessity — income inelastic (e.g., food, utilities)
YED > 1Luxury (income elastic) — demand rises more than proportionally with income (e.g., holidays, designer goods)
HL only — Significance of YED

For firms: A firm producing income-elastic goods (YED > 1) benefits greatly from economic growth — as incomes rise, demand grows rapidly. Firms can plan production and pricing accordingly.

For the economy: YED explains shifts in the sectoral structure of the economy over time. As incomes rise in a developing country, spending shifts from primary commodities (income inelastic: food, raw materials) toward manufactured goods and then toward services (income elastic). This explains the structural transformation from agriculture → industry → services seen in economic development.

Key points to remember
  • PED = %ΔQd ÷ %ΔP; always negative; usually expressed as absolute value
  • |PED| > 1 = elastic; |PED| < 1 = inelastic
  • Determinants: substitutes, necessity, income share, time
  • Elastic demand: price rise → TR falls; price fall → TR rises
  • Inelastic demand: price rise → TR rises; price fall → TR falls
  • YED = %ΔQd ÷ %Δincome; positive = normal; negative = inferior
  • YED > 1 = luxury; 0 < YED < 1 = necessity

Worked Example — PED Calculation

The price of petrol rises from $2.00 to $2.20 per litre. As a result, quantity demanded falls from 100,000 litres to 95,000 litres per week.

Step 1: Calculate % change in Qd

% ΔQd = (95,000 − 100,000) ÷ 100,000 × 100 = −5%

Step 2: Calculate % change in price

% ΔP = (2.20 − 2.00) ÷ 2.00 × 100 = +10%

Step 3: Calculate PED

PED = −5% ÷ 10% = −0.5 (|PED| = 0.5)
Demand is price inelastic — a 10% price rise causes only a 5% fall in quantity demanded.

Step 4: Effect on total revenue

TR before = 100,000 × $2.00 = $200,000
TR after = 95,000 × $2.20 = $209,000
TR increases — consistent with inelastic demand (price rise → TR rises).

Worked Example — YED Calculation

Consumer income rises by 8%. Demand for restaurant meals rises from 500 to 560 per week.

Step 1: Calculate % change in Qd

% ΔQd = (560 − 500) ÷ 500 × 100 = +12%

Step 2: Calculate YED

YED = +12% ÷ +8% = +1.5
YED > 1 → restaurant meals are a luxury good (income elastic, normal good).

Practice Questions
1. The price of cinema tickets rises from $12 to $15. Quantity demanded falls from 400 to 340 per week. Calculate the PED and state whether demand is elastic or inelastic.
Show answer
%ΔQd = (340 − 400) ÷ 400 × 100 = −15%
%ΔP = (15 − 12) ÷ 12 × 100 = +25%
PED = −15% ÷ 25% = −0.6 → |PED| = 0.6
Demand is price inelastic (|PED| < 1).
2. Explain why petrol tends to have a lower PED than luxury cars, using the determinants of PED.
Show answer
Petrol is more price inelastic because: (1) it has very few close substitutes for most car drivers; (2) it is a necessity — many people cannot easily reduce their petrol consumption; (3) in the short run, consumers cannot quickly change their commuting habits or buy a more fuel-efficient car. Luxury cars, by contrast, face more competition from other luxury goods, are not necessities, and represent a larger share of income — all factors increasing elasticity.
3. Using the PED–total revenue relationship, explain why a government may prefer to impose indirect taxes on inelastic goods.
Show answer
When demand is inelastic (|PED| < 1), a price rise causes a proportionally smaller fall in quantity demanded. Therefore, tax revenue is higher because the quantity sold does not fall much. For example, taxes on cigarettes, alcohol, and petrol generate large revenues because consumption does not fall dramatically in response to price increases. Taxing elastic goods would result in a large fall in quantity, reducing the tax base and yielding less revenue.
4. Income rises by 5%. Demand for a good falls from 200 to 180 units. Calculate YED and identify the type of good.
Show answer
%ΔQd = (180 − 200) ÷ 200 × 100 = −10%
YED = −10% ÷ 5% = −2
YED < 0 → this is an inferior good — demand falls as income rises.
