2.2

Supply

The law of supply, the supply curve, non-price determinants, and movements vs shifts.

You should be able to
  • State and explain the law of supply
  • Draw an upward-sloping supply curve and label it correctly
  • Explain the law of diminishing marginal returns and increasing marginal costs HL
  • Identify and explain the non-price determinants of supply
  • Distinguish between a movement along the supply curve and a shift of the supply curve

The Law of Supply

The law of supply states that, ceteris paribus, as the price of a good rises, the quantity supplied rises; as price falls, quantity supplied falls. There is a positive (direct) relationship between price and quantity supplied.

The supply curve slopes upward from left to right. Higher prices make production more profitable, incentivising producers to supply more.

Key term

Supply: The quantity of a good or service that producers are willing and able to offer for sale at various prices during a given time period, ceteris paribus.

HL only — Underlying assumptions

Law of diminishing marginal returns: As more of a variable factor (e.g., labour) is added to fixed factors (e.g., land, capital), each additional unit eventually adds less and less to output. This means output increases at a decreasing rate.

Increasing marginal costs: Because of diminishing returns, producing each additional unit costs more than the last. Firms therefore need higher prices to justify producing more — hence the upward-sloping supply curve.

Individual vs Market Supply

The individual supply curve shows how much one firm offers at each price. The market supply curve is the horizontal summation of all individual supply curves.

Non-Price Determinants of Supply

DeterminantShift right (increase in supply)Shift left (decrease in supply)
Costs of factors of productionFactor costs fall (e.g., wages, raw materials)Factor costs rise

Indirect taxesTax reduced (lowers production cost)Tax increased (raises production cost)

Key terms

Joint supply: Two goods produced together from the same process (e.g., beef and leather). A rise in demand for one increases supply of both.

Competitive supply: Goods that use the same resources to produce — a firm must choose between them (e.g., wheat vs barley on the same land).

Movements Along vs Shifts of the Supply Curve

Movement along the curve

Caused by a change in price of the good. Movement up the curve = extension in supply. Movement down = contraction in supply.

Shift of the curve

Caused by a change in any non-price determinant. A rightward shift = increase in supply (more supplied at every price). A leftward shift = decrease in supply.

Key points to remember
  • Law of supply: price up → quantity supplied up (positive relationship)
  • Supply curve slopes upward
  • Non-price factors shift the curve: factor costs, technology, taxes, subsidies, expectations, number of firms
  • Price change → movement along curve
  • Non-price change → shift of curve
  • HL: diminishing marginal returns → increasing marginal costs → upward slope

Practice Questions
1. State the law of supply and explain why the supply curve is upward sloping.
Show answer
The law of supply states that, ceteris paribus, as price rises, quantity supplied rises. The supply curve slopes upward because higher prices increase profit margins, making it worthwhile for existing producers to expand output and for new firms to enter the market. At the firm level, producing more involves rising marginal costs (due to diminishing returns), so higher prices are needed to justify additional production.
2. A drought destroys a large portion of the wheat harvest. Using a supply diagram, show and explain the effect on the market supply of wheat.
Show answer
A drought increases the cost of production and destroys existing output, reducing the ability of producers to supply wheat at every price level. The supply curve shifts to the left (S1 to S2), representing a decrease in supply. At any given price, less wheat is now available. This is a shift of the curve caused by a change in a non-price determinant (costs/availability of the factor land).
3. Explain, using examples, the difference between "joint supply" and "competitive supply."
Show answer
Joint supply exists when two goods are produced together in the same production process. For example, beef and leather both come from cattle — if more cattle are raised for beef, more leather is also produced as a byproduct. An increase in the price of beef shifts the supply of leather to the right. Competitive supply exists when producing more of one good means producing less of another, because they compete for the same limited resources. For example, a farmer with fixed land must choose between growing wheat or barley — if wheat prices rise, the farmer switches land to wheat, reducing the supply of barley.
