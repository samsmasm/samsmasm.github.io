2.1

Demand

The law of demand, the demand curve, non-price determinants, and movements vs shifts.

You should be able to
  • State and explain the law of demand
  • Draw a downward-sloping demand curve and label it correctly
  • Explain the income and substitution effects HL
  • Identify and explain the non-price determinants of demand
  • Distinguish between a movement along the demand curve and a shift of the demand curve

The Law of Demand

The law of demand states that, ceteris paribus, as the price of a good rises, the quantity demanded falls; as price falls, quantity demanded rises. There is an inverse (negative) relationship between price and quantity demanded.

Key term

Demand: The quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing and able to buy at various prices during a given time period, ceteris paribus.

The demand curve slopes downward from left to right, reflecting this inverse relationship.

Why the Demand Curve Slopes Downward

  • As price rises, consumers switch to substitutes (substitution effect)
  • As price rises, real income falls so consumers buy less (income effect)
  • As price falls, each additional unit purchased provides less extra satisfaction (diminishing marginal utility)
HL only — Underlying assumptions

Income effect: When price falls, consumers have more real purchasing power and can buy more of the good.

Substitution effect: When price falls, the good becomes relatively cheaper compared to substitutes, so consumers switch toward it.

Law of diminishing marginal utility: As consumption of a good increases, each additional unit yields progressively less additional satisfaction — so consumers are only willing to buy more if the price falls.

Individual vs Market Demand

The individual demand curve shows how much one consumer wishes to buy at each price. The market demand curve is the horizontal summation of all individual demand curves — it shows total quantity demanded by all consumers in the market at each price.

Non-Price Determinants of Demand

These factors cause the entire demand curve to shift — they change the quantity demanded at every price level.

DeterminantShift right (increase in demand)Shift left (decrease in demand)
IncomeRise in income (for normal goods)Fall in income (for normal goods); rise in income for inferior goods
Tastes and preferencesGood becomes more fashionableGood becomes less fashionable
Future price expectationsConsumers expect price to rise (buy now)Consumers expect price to fall (wait)
Price of substitutesPrice of substitute rises (switch to this good)Price of substitute falls (switch away)
Price of complementsPrice of complement falls (buy more of both)Price of complement rises (buy less of both)
Number of consumersPopulation grows or market expandsPopulation falls or market shrinks
Key terms

Normal good: Demand rises as income rises (positive income effect).

Inferior good: Demand falls as income rises — consumers switch to better alternatives.

Substitutes: Goods that can replace each other (e.g., butter and margarine). A rise in the price of one increases demand for the other.

Complements: Goods consumed together (e.g., printers and ink). A rise in the price of one decreases demand for the other.

Movements Along vs Shifts of the Demand Curve

Movement along the curve

Caused by a change in price of the good itself. Movement up the curve = contraction in demand. Movement down = extension in demand. The curve itself does not move.

Shift of the curve

Caused by a change in any non-price determinant. A rightward shift = increase in demand (more demanded at every price). A leftward shift = decrease in demand.

Exam tip

A very common error is to say "demand increased because price fell." Price changes cause movements along the curve, not shifts. Only non-price factors shift the curve. Use "quantity demanded" for movements and "demand" for shifts.

Key points to remember
  • Law of demand: price up → quantity demanded down (inverse relationship)
  • Demand curve slopes downward
  • Non-price factors shift the curve: income, tastes, related goods prices, expectations, number of consumers
  • Price change → movement along curve
  • Non-price change → shift of curve
  • Normal good: demand rises with income; inferior good: demand falls with income

Practice Questions
1. State the law of demand and explain why the demand curve is downward sloping.
Show answer
The law of demand states that, ceteris paribus, as price rises, quantity demanded falls. The curve slopes downward because: (1) as price falls, consumers can afford more (income effect); (2) the good becomes relatively cheaper than substitutes (substitution effect); and (3) as more is consumed, each additional unit gives less satisfaction, so consumers will only buy more if price falls (diminishing marginal utility).
2. Using a demand diagram, show the effect on the market for butter of a significant rise in the price of margarine. Explain your diagram.
Show answer
Margarine is a substitute for butter. When the price of margarine rises, consumers switch away from margarine toward butter. This increases demand for butter — the demand curve for butter shifts to the right (D1 to D2). At every price, a greater quantity of butter is demanded. This is a shift of the curve, not a movement along it.
3. Explain the difference between a "movement along" and a "shift of" the demand curve. Give one cause of each.
Show answer
A movement along the demand curve occurs when the price of the good itself changes — for example, if the price of coffee rises from $3 to $5, there is a contraction in quantity demanded (movement up the curve). A shift of the demand curve occurs when a non-price determinant changes — for example, if consumer incomes rise, demand for normal goods increases and the entire curve shifts rightward.
4. Distinguish between a normal good and an inferior good. Give one example of each.
Show answer
A normal good is one for which demand rises as consumer income rises (positive income elasticity of demand) — for example, restaurant meals or smartphones. An inferior good is one for which demand falls as income rises, because consumers switch to better alternatives — for example, instant noodles or budget supermarket own-brands.
