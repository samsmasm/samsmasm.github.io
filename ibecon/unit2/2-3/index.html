2.3

Competitive Market Equilibrium

Market equilibrium, the price mechanism, consumer and producer surplus, and allocative efficiency.

You should be able to
  • Define market equilibrium and show it on a diagram
  • Explain how markets adjust through the price mechanism
  • Explain the functions of the price mechanism: signalling, incentive, and rationing
  • Define and show consumer surplus, producer surplus, and social/community surplus on a diagram
  • Explain why social surplus is maximised at competitive equilibrium (allocative efficiency)
  • Calculate consumer and producer surplus from a diagram HL

Market Equilibrium

Equilibrium is the price at which quantity demanded equals quantity supplied — the market clears. There is no tendency for price to change unless an external factor shifts demand or supply.

Equilibrium price
The price at which Qd = Qs. Also called the "market-clearing price."
Equilibrium quantity
The quantity bought and sold at the equilibrium price.
Excess demand (shortage)
When price is below equilibrium: Qd > Qs. Upward pressure on price.
Excess supply (surplus)
When price is above equilibrium: Qs > Qd. Downward pressure on price.

The Price Mechanism

Markets achieve equilibrium through the price mechanism, which performs three functions:

FunctionExplanation
SignallingPrice changes communicate information to buyers and sellers. Rising prices signal that a good is relatively scarce; falling prices signal abundance.
IncentivePrice changes create incentives to act. High prices incentivise producers to increase supply and consumers to reduce consumption. Low prices do the opposite.
RationingPrices allocate scarce goods to those willing and able to pay. Higher prices ration goods to those who value them most (at least in terms of willingness to pay).

Shifts in Demand and Supply — New Equilibrium

When demand or supply shifts, the market moves to a new equilibrium. The table below summarises the key outcomes:

ChangeEffect on equilibrium priceEffect on equilibrium quantity
Demand increases (D shifts right)RisesRises
Demand decreases (D shifts left)FallsFalls
Supply increases (S shifts right)FallsRises
Supply decreases (S shifts left)RisesFalls

Consumer Surplus, Producer Surplus, and Social Surplus

Consumer surplus (CS)
The difference between the maximum price a consumer is willing to pay and the actual price paid. Shown as the area above the equilibrium price and below the demand curve.
Producer surplus (PS)
The difference between the actual price received and the minimum price a producer would accept. Shown as the area below the equilibrium price and above the supply curve.
Social (community) surplus
The sum of consumer surplus and producer surplus. It measures total welfare in the market: CS + PS.

Allocative Efficiency

At competitive market equilibrium, social surplus is maximised. This is the condition for allocative efficiency:

  • Social surplus is as large as possible — no reallocation can make someone better off without making someone else worse off
  • At equilibrium, marginal benefit (MB) = marginal cost (MC), equivalently Price = MB = MC
HL only — Calculating surplus from a diagram

Consumer surplus and producer surplus are areas of triangles on the supply and demand diagram.

For a straight-line demand and supply curve meeting at equilibrium (P*, Q*):

  • CS = ½ × base × height = ½ × Q* × (Pmax − P*)
  • PS = ½ × base × height = ½ × Q* × (P* − Pmin)

where Pmax is the demand-curve intercept (maximum willingness to pay) and Pmin is the supply-curve intercept (minimum acceptable price).

Key points to remember
  • Equilibrium: Qd = Qs; market clears
  • Price mechanism: signalling, incentive, rationing
  • Shortage (Qd > Qs) → price rises; Surplus (Qs > Qd) → price falls
  • CS = area above price, below demand curve
  • PS = area below price, above supply curve
  • Social surplus = CS + PS, maximised at equilibrium
  • Allocative efficiency: P = MB = MC at equilibrium

Worked Example — Calculating Surplus HL

The market for apples has the following equations: Demand: P = 10 − Q; Supply: P = 2 + Q.

Step 1: Find equilibrium

Set Qd = Qs:
10 − Q = 2 + Q
8 = 2Q
Q* = 4, P* = 6

Step 2: Find the demand-curve intercept (Pmax)

When Q = 0: P = 10 − 0 = 10

Step 3: Find the supply-curve intercept (Pmin)

When Q = 0: P = 2 + 0 = 2

Step 4: Calculate CS and PS

CS = ½ × Q* × (Pmax − P*) = ½ × 4 × (10 − 6) = ½ × 4 × 4 = 8
PS = ½ × Q* × (P* − Pmin) = ½ × 4 × (6 − 2) = ½ × 4 × 4 = 8
Social surplus = CS + PS = 16

Practice Questions
1. Explain the three functions of the price mechanism in allocating resources.
Show answer
Signalling: Price changes communicate information — a rise in price signals relative scarcity, a fall signals relative abundance. Producers and consumers use these signals to make decisions. Incentive: Price changes motivate behaviour — high prices incentivise firms to increase supply and consumers to reduce consumption. Rationing: Prices allocate scarce goods among competing users — only those willing and able to pay the market price receive the good.
2. Using a diagram, explain what happens in a market where the current price is above the equilibrium price.
Show answer
When price is above equilibrium, quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded — there is excess supply (a surplus). Unsold inventories accumulate. To clear this stock, producers lower prices. As price falls, quantity supplied falls and quantity demanded rises, until the market returns to equilibrium where Qd = Qs.
3. Explain why social surplus is maximised at the competitive market equilibrium. Use the concepts of marginal benefit and marginal cost.
Show answer
At competitive equilibrium, price equals both marginal benefit (MB) for consumers and marginal cost (MC) for producers: P = MB = MC. This means the last unit produced is exactly as valuable to consumers as it costs to produce — no further mutually beneficial exchange is possible. Any output below equilibrium would leave unexploited gains from trade; any output above equilibrium would involve producing units whose cost exceeds their value. Therefore, social surplus (CS + PS) is maximised at equilibrium — this is the condition for allocative efficiency.
4. HL The market for coffee has: Demand P = 8 − Q; Supply P = 2 + Q. Calculate (a) the equilibrium price and quantity, (b) consumer surplus, and (c) producer surplus.
Show answer
(a) Set 8 − Q = 2 + Q → 6 = 2Q → Q* = 3, P* = 5
(b) CS = ½ × 3 × (8 − 5) = ½ × 3 × 3 = 4.5
(c) PS = ½ × 3 × (5 − 2) = ½ × 3 × 3 = 4.5
