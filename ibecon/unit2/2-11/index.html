2.11 HL

Market Failure — Market Power

Perfect competition, monopoly, oligopoly, monopolistic competition, and profit maximisation.

HL only

Profit Maximisation

All firms maximise profit where:

Profit maximisation condition
MC = MR (producing the last unit where marginal cost equals marginal revenue)

Equivalently: maximise TR − TC (total revenue minus total cost).

  • Abnormal (supernormal) profit: AR > AC (price above average cost)
  • Normal profit: AR = AC (zero economic profit — just covering opportunity cost)
  • Loss: AR < AC (price below average cost)

Note: AR = P = D for a firm (average revenue equals the demand curve).

Perfect Competition

In perfect competition, the firm is a price taker — it must accept the market price. Its demand curve is perfectly elastic (horizontal): P = D = AR = MR.

Short run

Firms can earn abnormal profit, normal profit, or make a loss. At profit-maximising output (MC = MR), if P > AC → abnormal profit; if P = AC → normal profit; if P < AC → loss.

Long run

Abnormal profits attract new firms (free entry). Supply increases, market price falls, until P = AC = AR and all firms earn normal profit. Similarly, losses drive firms to exit until price rises back to normal profit.

Long-run equilibrium in perfect competition: P = MC = AC — allocatively and productively efficient.

Monopoly

A monopolist faces a downward-sloping demand curve — it must lower price to sell more (price maker). The MR curve is below the AR (demand) curve.

Profit maximisation: MC = MR, at output QM. The monopolist charges PM (read from the demand curve at QM).

Allocative inefficiency in monopoly

The monopolist produces where MC = MR, but this means P > MC (price exceeds marginal cost). Since P = MB for consumers, and MB > MC, the market is under-producing relative to the social optimum. There is a welfare loss (deadweight loss triangle).

Natural monopoly

A natural monopoly occurs when a single firm can supply the entire market at lower cost than multiple firms — long-run average costs fall continuously over the relevant range of output (economies of scale). Examples: utilities, railways. This creates a case for government regulation or ownership.

Oligopoly

Oligopoly is characterised by interdependence — each firm's decisions affect and are affected by rivals' decisions. This leads to strategic behaviour.

Collusion vs non-collusion

  • Collusive oligopoly: Firms cooperate (explicitly — a cartel — or tacitly) to act like a monopolist, restrict output and raise prices. Maximises joint profit.
  • Non-collusive oligopoly: Firms compete strategically — may lead to price wars.

Game theory

A simple payoff matrix shows that each firm has an incentive to cheat on a collusive agreement (dominant strategy), even though both firms would be better off if both cooperated — a prisoner's dilemma. This makes cartels unstable.

Concentration ratio

The concentration ratio measures the market share of the top n firms (e.g., CR4 = combined market share of the 4 largest firms). A high concentration ratio indicates significant market power.

Monopolistic Competition

Monopolistic competition combines features of both monopoly and perfect competition: many firms, free entry/exit, but each has a differentiated product (unique brand, location, style).

Short run

Firms can earn abnormal profit (like a monopolist, with a downward-sloping demand curve for their differentiated product).

Long run

Abnormal profits attract entry. New competitors take market share, shifting each firm's demand curve left. In the long run: P = AC (normal profit), but P > MC — allocatively inefficient, with some welfare loss.

Trade-off: monopolistic competition leads to some inefficiency, but also greater product variety than perfect competition.

Government Responses to Market Power

PolicyHow it works
Legislation and regulationCompetition law prohibits anti-competitive behaviour, price-fixing cartels, and abusive monopoly practices
Government ownershipNatural monopolies (utilities) may be nationalised and run as public enterprises to prevent price gouging
FinesCompetition authorities fine firms found guilty of cartel behaviour or abuse of dominant position
Key points to remember
  • Profit max: MC = MR; abnormal profit if AR > AC
  • Perfect competition: price taker; long run: P = MC = AC (efficient)
  • Monopoly: price maker; P > MC → allocative inefficiency + welfare loss
  • Natural monopoly: LRAC falls over entire market — one firm is most efficient
  • Oligopoly: interdependence; incentive to collude but also to cheat
  • Monopolistic competition: short run abnormal profit; long run normal profit; P > MC always

Practice Questions HL
1. Using a diagram, explain why a monopolist produces at an allocatively inefficient level of output.
Show answer
A monopolist profit-maximises where MC = MR, producing output QM and charging price PM. Since the demand curve is above the MR curve, PM > MR = MC. Allocative efficiency requires P = MC (MB = MC). Because PM > MC, the monopolist is underproducing relative to the socially optimal output. The welfare loss is the triangle between the demand curve (MB), the MC curve, and the vertical line at QM.
2. Using a game theory payoff matrix, explain why oligopolists have an incentive to cheat on a collusive agreement.
Show answer
Consider two firms each choosing whether to cooperate (keep prices high) or cheat (undercut). If both cooperate, both earn moderate profit. If Firm A cheats while B cooperates, A earns high profit (takes B's customers) and B earns low profit. If both cheat, both earn low profit (price war). Each firm reasons: "If B cooperates, I earn more by cheating. If B cheats, I also earn more by cheating (limit losses)." Cheating is the dominant strategy for both — yet both cheating leads to a worse outcome than both cooperating. This prisoner's dilemma makes cartels inherently unstable.
3. Explain why monopolistic competition is less allocatively efficient than perfect competition, but may still offer advantages for consumers.
Show answer
In monopolistic competition, even in the long run, P > MC — the firm charges a price above marginal cost because it has some market power over its differentiated product. This means there is a welfare loss compared with perfect competition (where P = MC in long-run equilibrium). However, the differentiation that creates this inefficiency also provides consumers with greater product variety — different brands, styles, locations, and features to suit different preferences. Many consumers value choice even if it comes with slightly higher prices.
