2.10 HL

Market Failure — Asymmetric Information

Adverse selection, moral hazard, and government and private responses.

HL only

  • Distinguish between adverse selection and moral hazard with examples
  • Explain government responses: legislation, regulation, provision of information
  • Explain private responses: signalling and screening

Asymmetric Information

Asymmetric information occurs when one party in a transaction has more or better information than the other. This prevents markets from operating efficiently — it can lead to markets breaking down, wrong prices, or the wrong quantities being traded.

The two main consequences of asymmetric information are adverse selection and moral hazard.

Adverse Selection

Adverse selection occurs before a transaction — it arises because one party has private information about their own characteristics that the other cannot observe. This causes a disproportionate number of "bad risks" or "low quality" options to participate in the market.

Classic example: The used car market (Akerlof's "Market for Lemons")

Sellers of used cars know whether their car is good or a "lemon" (poor quality), but buyers cannot easily tell the difference. Buyers therefore offer only an average price. Good-quality car owners, knowing their car is worth more, withdraw from the market. Only sellers of bad-quality cars (lemons) remain — the market is "adversely selected" toward poor quality. At the extreme, the market collapses entirely.

Other examples

  • Health insurance: People who know they are high-risk (sick) are more likely to buy insurance. Insurers, unable to distinguish risk levels, charge average premiums. Low-risk (healthy) people find this too expensive and drop out. The insured pool becomes increasingly high-risk, pushing premiums up further.
  • Labour markets: Firms cannot easily assess worker productivity before hiring. If wages are set at an average level, highly productive workers may seek employers who can observe their quality, leaving firms with a below-average pool of applicants.

Moral Hazard

Moral hazard occurs after a transaction — when one party changes their behaviour because they no longer bear the full consequences of their actions, due to the other party's inability to monitor them.

Examples

  • Insurance: Once insured, individuals may take less care (e.g., a fully insured driver may drive more recklessly). The insurer cannot perfectly monitor behaviour.
  • Banking: If banks believe governments will bail them out in a crisis, they may take excessive risks — the taxpayer bears the downside. This was a major factor in the 2008 financial crisis.
  • Employment: An employee whose effort is difficult to monitor may work less hard after being hired (shirking).

Responses to Asymmetric Information

Government responses

ResponseHow it addresses the problemExample
Legislation and regulationMandatory disclosure requirements force sellers to reveal information; consumer protection laws prevent exploitationFood labelling laws, financial product disclosure requirements
Provision of informationGovernment provides free information to reduce the knowledge gapPublic health campaigns, product safety ratings, school inspection reports

Private responses

ResponseDirection of informationExample
SignallingThe informed party sends a credible signal to the less-informed partyJob applicants use university degrees as signals of ability; firms offer warranties to signal product quality
ScreeningThe less-informed party gathers information by designing mechanisms that reveal private informationInsurers offer multiple policy options at different prices to reveal customers' risk levels; employers use probationary periods or interviews to learn about workers
Key points to remember
  • Asymmetric information: one party knows more than the other
  • Adverse selection: before transaction; disproportionate "bad risks" participate
  • Moral hazard: after transaction; behaviour changes due to reduced consequences
  • Government: legislation, regulation, information provision
  • Private: signalling (informed party signals) vs screening (uninformed party investigates)

Practice Questions HL
1. Explain the difference between adverse selection and moral hazard. Use the insurance industry as a context for both.
Show answer
Adverse selection occurs before the insurance contract is signed. High-risk individuals know they are high-risk and are more likely to seek insurance. If the insurer cannot distinguish risk levels and charges average premiums, low-risk individuals may not buy insurance (too expensive for them), leaving the pool increasingly high-risk — the insurer has "adversely selected" its customers. Moral hazard occurs after the contract. Once insured, the insured party may take less care (e.g., leave valuables unattended, drive recklessly) because the insurer bears the cost of any loss. The insurer cannot fully monitor behaviour, creating the moral hazard.
2. Distinguish between "signalling" and "screening" as private responses to asymmetric information. Give one example of each.
Show answer
Signalling is when the party with more information sends a credible signal to the less-informed party. For example, a job applicant obtains a university degree to signal ability to employers — the degree is valuable as a signal because it is costly to obtain, so only genuinely able candidates find it worthwhile. Screening is when the less-informed party designs mechanisms to reveal private information. For example, an insurance company offers several policy options with different premium/excess combinations — high-risk customers will choose low-excess (high premium) policies while low-risk customers choose high-excess, revealing their risk type to the insurer.
