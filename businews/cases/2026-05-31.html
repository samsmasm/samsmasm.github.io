Inside Information or Just Being Smart? The Polymarket Insider Trading Test

A Google engineer allegedly turned his employer's secret data into $1.2 million in trading profits. Now a landmark legal case is asking a question nobody has answered yet: can you commit insider trading on a prediction market?

Source: euronews.com →

Background

Polymarket is an online prediction market where users bet real money on the outcomes of future events, from elections to economic data releases. Google is one of the world's largest technology companies, whose search engine processes billions of queries daily, generating vast internal data about what people are searching for in real time. That internal data, known as search trend data, is proprietary, meaning it belongs to Google and is not available to the public.

What Happened

  • A Google engineer was arrested in May 2025, accused of accessing confidential internal search trend data to predict the outcomes of events listed on Polymarket.
  • The engineer allegedly used those predictions to place trades on Polymarket, accumulating profits of approximately $1.2 million.
  • Prosecutors argued this constituted insider trading, the illegal practice of trading on material, non-public information that gives one party an unfair advantage over others.
  • The case is considered a landmark because prediction markets like Polymarket have never previously been subject to the same regulatory scrutiny as traditional financial markets such as stock exchanges.

Data Snapshot

  • $1.2 million: the alleged profit made by the engineer through trades on Polymarket using Google's internal data.
  • Billions of queries processed daily by Google's search engine, generating the proprietary trend data at the centre of the case.
  • 0: the number of successful insider trading prosecutions involving a prediction market prior to this case, underlining how legally untested the territory is.

The Tension

At the heart of this case is a collision between two business concepts: information asymmetry, where one party in a transaction knows something the other does not, and market regulation, the legal framework designed to keep markets fair. On a stock exchange, using confidential corporate data to trade is clearly illegal. But Polymarket operates in a grey zone: it has historically been treated as a novelty platform rather than a regulated financial market. The engineer's defence could argue he simply used his skills and access cleverly. Prosecutors counter that the information was stolen from his employer and used for personal gain, raising serious questions about employee ethics, intellectual property, and who ultimately gets to define what counts as a financial market.

Outside View

Legal analysts have pointed out that this case could force regulators to formally classify prediction markets as financial instruments, a move the industry has long resisted. Critics of platforms like Polymarket argue that the absence of regulation has always created the conditions for exactly this kind of abuse. Supporters of prediction markets, however, warn that heavy-handed regulation could destroy the genuine information value these platforms provide, since aggregating the bets of many informed people can produce surprisingly accurate forecasts.

Reflection Questions

  1. What is the difference between insider trading and simply being better informed than other market participants, and where should the legal line be drawn?
  2. What would you need to know about how Polymarket is classified by financial regulators to decide whether existing insider trading laws even apply here?
  3. How might this case affect Google's internal data governance policies and its relationship of trust with employees?
  4. To what extent should prediction markets be regulated in the same way as traditional stock or commodity exchanges?
  5. What ethical responsibilities does an employee have toward their employer's proprietary data, even if using it does not clearly break the law?

Extension

Research how the US Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) has previously attempted to regulate Polymarket, and evaluate whether its existing legal powers would be sufficient to prosecute this case.
