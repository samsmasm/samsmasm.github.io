To the Moon -- But Who Pays? SpaceX's IPO and the Price of a Vision

SpaceX wants to raise more money from public investors than almost any company in history. The catch? It is still losing money. So why would anyone buy in?

Source: euronews.com →

Background

SpaceX (Space Exploration Technologies Corp.) is an American aerospace company founded in 2002 by Elon Musk with the stated goal of making humanity a multi-planetary species. It designs, manufactures, and launches rockets and spacecraft, most notably the Falcon 9 and the Starship rocket. Beyond rockets, SpaceX operates Starlink, a commercial satellite internet service that beams broadband connectivity to customers in remote areas worldwide using a vast constellation of low-orbit satellites. SpaceX has historically been a private company, meaning its shares are not available for ordinary members of the public to buy on a stock exchange.

What Happened

  • SpaceX is planning an Initial Public Offering (IPO) -- the first time a private company sells shares to the general public on a stock exchange -- that could raise approximately $75 billion, potentially making it the largest IPO in history.
  • The company intends to use the funds raised to finance its most ambitious missions, including a return to the Moon and an eventual crewed mission to Mars.
  • Starlink, SpaceX's satellite internet division, has become a significant and growing source of revenue, providing the company's strongest financial performance.
  • Despite Starlink's success, SpaceX as a whole continues to report net losses, meaning its total costs still exceed its total revenues.
  • Elon Musk's other ventures, including the social media platform X (formerly Twitter) and the artificial intelligence company xAI, are also reported to be loss-making, raising questions about Musk's capacity to manage multiple struggling businesses simultaneously.

Data Snapshot

  • Target IPO fundraise: approximately $75 billion (around €64.5 billion), which would rank among the largest IPOs in stock market history.
  • Starlink is SpaceX's primary revenue-generating division, providing satellite broadband to customers globally -- its subscriber base and revenues have grown substantially in recent years.
  • SpaceX as a whole continues to report net losses despite Starlink's performance, meaning total company expenditure exceeds total income.
  • Elon Musk's other companies, X and xAI, are also reported to be loss-making at the time of the planned IPO.

The Tension

At the heart of this story is one of the oldest tensions in business finance: the gap between vision and viability. An IPO is not simply a fundraising exercise. It is a company making a public promise to shareholders that their investment will, eventually, generate a return. When investors buy shares, they are buying a claim on future profits. SpaceX is asking the public to pay an enormous sum for a company that has not yet demonstrated it can make a consistent profit. This is not unusual for high-growth technology firms -- Amazon lost money for years before becoming extraordinarily profitable -- but the scale of the ask here is remarkable. A $75 billion raise would represent one of the largest capital injections in stock market history.

The tension deepens when you look inside SpaceX's revenue structure. Starlink is performing well and represents a credible, scalable business model: subscription fees from internet customers provide predictable, recurring revenue. This is exactly the kind of income stream that reassures investors. But Starlink's profits are, in effect, subsidising the far more expensive and speculative work of deep-space exploration. Moon and Mars missions generate prestige and contracts (including significant NASA funding), but they are not yet commercial profit centres in the way Starlink is. Investors buying into the IPO need to decide whether they are funding a telecommunications company with rocket capabilities, or a space exploration company that happens to run a profitable internet service on the side. The answer to that question significantly changes the risk profile of the investment.

There is also a broader strategic concern around Elon Musk himself. In corporate governance (the system of rules and practices by which a company is directed and controlled), a key risk factor is over-dependence on a single leader. Musk is simultaneously running SpaceX, Tesla, X, and xAI -- and both X and xAI are reportedly loss-making. If investors in a SpaceX IPO are partly buying confidence in Musk's leadership, they must also weigh the question of whether his attention and resources are too thinly spread. Public shareholders, unlike private backers, will have the right to scrutinise this through mandatory financial disclosures and, in some structures, voting rights. That transparency cuts both ways: it may attract institutional investors who want visibility, while also exposing pressures that were previously managed away from public view.

Outside View

Many financial analysts and journalists are sceptical about whether the $75 billion figure reflects the company's current financial fundamentals, or whether it is inflated by the extraordinary public profile of Elon Musk and the romantic appeal of space exploration. Some commentators argue that Starlink, if valued as a standalone telecommunications business, would support a strong but more modest valuation -- and that the Mars mission narrative is doing significant work in justifying the premium being asked of investors. Others point to the concentration of Musk-linked businesses all seeking capital at the same time as a structural concern. There is also a school of thought that the IPO is being timed strategically to take advantage of current investor enthusiasm for AI and space technology, rather than because the company's financials have reached a natural inflection point. Supporters counter that SpaceX holds a dominant position in commercial launch services and that Starlink's growth trajectory alone could justify a bold valuation.

Reflection Questions

  1. SpaceX is growing rapidly but still making a net loss. Using your knowledge of investment and sources of finance, explain why investors might still consider buying shares in the IPO.
  2. What would you need to know about SpaceX's financial structure -- particularly the relationship between Starlink revenues and the costs of its space exploration programmes -- before deciding whether the $75 billion valuation is justified?
  3. Evaluate the view that SpaceX's decision to go public is driven more by financial necessity than by strategic choice. To what extent do you agree?
  4. How might the IPO change the way SpaceX is managed? Consider the impact of moving from private to public ownership on decision-making, accountability, and long-term strategy.
  5. Elon Musk is simultaneously leading several loss-making companies. To what extent is leadership concentration a significant risk factor for potential SpaceX investors, and how might this affect the IPO's success?

Extension

Research the IPO histories of two other high-profile technology companies that listed while making a loss -- such as Amazon (1997) or Uber (2019) -- and compare their post-IPO financial trajectories. What conditions seem to determine whether a loss-making IPO ultimately rewards or disappoints its early investors? Write a one-page briefing note advising a hypothetical retail investor on whether to buy SpaceX shares at launch.
