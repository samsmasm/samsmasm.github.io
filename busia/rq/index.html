Stage 2

Find Your Research Question

This is where most students get stuck. Two activities to get you unstuck.

A good research question is the foundation of a good IA. Too broad and you cannot go deep enough. Too narrow and you cannot find enough evidence. Too vague and you cannot reach a real conclusion. This stage is about finding something specific, genuinely interesting, and actually researchable.

Your research question must be about a real, named business. It must connect clearly to one of the four key concepts (Change, Creativity, Ethics, Sustainability). And it must be something you can actually investigate - meaning you can get hold of real supporting documents.

What makes a good RQ?

A good BM IA research question:

  • Names a specific business (not "a company" or "businesses in general")
  • Focuses on a real decision, change, problem, or challenge that business has faced
  • Connects naturally to one of the four key concepts
  • Is genuinely open - you do not already know the answer
  • Can be investigated using 3-5 real documents

Some examples

From the IB:

"Should company Y change its manufacturing to outsourcing?" Could examine operations management and human resource management using change as a conceptual lens.
"How can airline X successfully target segment Y?" Could examine market segmentation, promotion, and financial measures using sustainability as a conceptual lens.

From Sam:

"Should [a local family-run bakery] introduce an e-commerce platform to increase its market share?" Could examine marketing (e-commerce, market share), operations management (delivery logistics), and finance (investment appraisal) using change or creativity as a conceptual lens.
"Should Nintendo stop making Rated M games and focus exclusively on kid-friendly content to maintain its brand image?" Could examine marketing (branding, product positioning), corporate strategy (Ansoff Matrix), and stakeholder impact using ethics or change as a conceptual lens.
"To what extent should the Coca-Cola Company change its bottling processes in [specific country/region] to be more sustainable?" Could examine operations management (production processes), marketing (green branding), and finance (cost-benefit analysis) using sustainability as a conceptual lens.

Notice that good RQs name a specific business, imply a specific concept, and are phrased as questions that actually require thinking to answer. The bakery example shows that a local or lesser-known business can work well - it is easier to gather primary evidence and there is less risk of simply summarising what has already been written online.

Activity 2: The News Hunt

If Activity 1 has not produced a clear candidate, or you want to find a business with strong publicly available evidence, use this four-step process.

Activity

News Hunt: finding a researchable business

1Pick a sector you find interesting. Retail, tech, food and beverage, sport, fashion, healthcare - anything. Write it down.

2Search for recent news in that sector. Use Google News, BBC Business, or a business newspaper. Search terms like: "[sector] company decision 2023", "[sector] ethics controversy", "[sector] sustainability strategy". You are looking for a story about a specific named business that has actually done something - not just a trend article.

3Check whether documents exist. Before you commit to a business, spend 10 minutes checking: Is there a company annual report or sustainability report? Are there two or three news articles about this specific decision? Could you gather a primary source (interview, survey, company statement)? If the answer to at least two of these is yes, it is worth pursuing.

4Draft a rough RQ. Write it in the format: "To what extent has [business] [done something specific] in order to [achieve what]?" or "How effective has [business]'s [strategy/decision] been in [achieving what]?" It does not need to be perfect yet. You will refine it as you gather your documents.

Local vs. international, private vs. public

The type of business you choose has a direct impact on what evidence you can get hold of. Think about this before you commit.

Local
Good for: collecting primary data - interviews, surveys, visits are much more accessible.

Harder: secondary data is limited or non-existent. Getting a good balance of documents is difficult.
International
Good for: secondary data - news coverage, reports, and analysis are usually plentiful.

Harder: getting primary data. But the depth of secondary sources often outweighs this.
Private
Good for: primary research if you have a connection to the business.

Harder: secondary data is scarce. Private companies do not publish financial reports.
Public
Good for: secondary data - annual reports, financial statements, and press coverage are usually abundant.

Harder: primary access is unlikely, but the secondary data advantage usually more than compensates.

There is no single right answer. A large international public company gives you the most secondary evidence but the least opportunity for primary research. A local private business is the opposite. The sweet spot for many students is a mid-sized business with some public profile - enough secondary coverage to build on, and realistic prospects for a primary source.

Before you lock in your RQ

Run your draft research question past Sam. The most common problems are:

  • Too descriptive: "What did Nike do about sweatshops?" is a research topic, not a research question. It leads to a report, not an analysis.
  • Too broad: "How has Apple been innovative?" gives you no way to scope the project - Apple has been innovative in too many ways.
  • No concept angle: If you cannot immediately see which of the four concepts fits, the question might not be right for BM IA.
  • No evidence available: If you cannot find at least three relevant documents within 20 minutes, you will struggle to build the analysis.
Get Sam's approval before moving on. Once your RQ is signed off, the rest of the project has a foundation to build on. Without a clear RQ, the supporting documents, the tool choice, and the structure all become harder.
