Stage 4

Supporting Documents

The evidence base for your research. Aim for five.

The supporting documents sit in your appendix and form the evidential foundation of your IA. They are not just references: they are the raw material you will actually analyse. The examiner will check them. Every factual claim in your commentary should trace back to one of these documents.

The rules

  • 3 to 5 documents - aim for 5. Five is almost always better than four or three. More documents means more evidence to draw on, more perspectives, and stronger breadth for Criterion B. Only go lower if the documents you have are genuinely stronger without the extras. Note: fewer than three or more than five results in a score of only 1 mark for Criterion B, regardless of quality.
  • Published within the last three years of your submission date. Check the date on every document.
  • Primary and/or secondary. You are not required to have both, but the IB expects a range. An IA built entirely on secondary sources is weaker than one that includes something you gathered yourself.
  • A range of views. The IB strongly recommends that supporting documents present a range of ideas and perspectives. Using only company-published materials may lack the objectivity needed for high marks.
  • Directly relevant to your RQ. If a document does not help you answer the research question, it should not be in your appendix.

Primary vs. secondary sources

Primary
You generate these yourself.

- Interview with a manager, employee, or customer
- Survey you designed and distributed
- Company document obtained directly (price list, internal report)
- Field observation notes
- Personal communication from the business
Secondary
Published by someone else.

- News article from a reputable outlet
- Company annual report or sustainability report
- Industry market research report
- Academic journal article
- Government or NGO data
- Transcript of a video/audio file (max one per IA)

You can use all primary, all secondary, or a mix - the IB does not mandate a ratio. In practice, a mix is almost always stronger. Five secondary news articles all saying the same thing scores poorly on Criterion B. A primary source - an interview, a survey, a document obtained directly from the business - adds something that cannot be found online.

What makes a strong supporting document?

  • It contains specific, usable data: numbers, quotes, decisions, timelines
  • It is from a credible, identifiable source
  • It is clearly dated within the three-year window
  • It relates directly and specifically to your research question - not just your topic in general
  • It would be difficult to explain your analysis without it

Common mistakes

Using Wikipedia or blog posts. These are not acceptable sources. Use the original source that Wikipedia itself cites.
Five documents that all say the same thing. Diversity of source and perspective strengthens your analysis. If all five documents come from the company's own PR, your evaluation is one-sided.
Collecting documents before you know your RQ. This leads to documents that are loosely related but not genuinely useful. Know your RQ first, then collect evidence to answer it.
Forgetting to label them. Each document must be labelled clearly in the appendix (Document 1, Document 2...) and referenced by that label in the commentary.

Conducting an interview

If you are interviewing someone at the business (strongly recommended), a few practical points:

  • Send questions in advance so the interviewee can prepare
  • Ask open questions: "How has..." "To what extent has..." "What were the main challenges..."
  • Record the interview (with permission) or take detailed notes
  • Write it up as a transcript or structured summary - that becomes your supporting document
  • Get the interviewee's name, role, and the date
The documents are your evidence, not your IA. They go in the appendix. Your job in the commentary is to use them - reference specific data, quote key statements, and build analysis from what they show.
