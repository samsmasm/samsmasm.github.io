# What Examiners Are Looking For

Reference | A criterion-by-criterion guide to each mark level

## The Big Picture

Your commentary is marked on five criteria totalling 14 marks. The examiner reads only 800 words (diagram labels and legends do not count). Everything beyond word 800 is invisible.

Across every criterion, one principle matters more than anything else: ground your work in the article. The strongest commentaries use data, quotes, and specifics from the article to drive diagrams, analysis, and evaluation. The weakest read like textbook essays that happen to mention an article.

You are writing about this article, not about economics in general.

| Criterion | What it assesses | Max |
|---|---|---|
| A | Diagrams: construct and explain | 3 |
| B | Terminology: use terms correctly throughout | 2 |
| C | Application and Analysis: apply theory in context | 3 |
| D | Key Concept: link a key concept to the article | 3 |
| E | Evaluation: make supported, balanced judgments | 3 |
| Total | | 14 |

---

## Criterion A: Diagrams (3 marks)

**3/3: What to do:**
- Include diagram(s) relevant to your article. If the article covers two distinct economic issues, include a diagram for each.
- Label axes and curves with the specific product and context from the article. If the article gives actual prices or values, use them.
- Explain each diagram fully in your text: what the curves represent, what the shift or area shows, and how it connects to what the article describes.

**2/3: What to do at minimum:**
- Include at least one relevant, correctly labelled diagram.
- Provide some explanation of what the diagram shows.

**What pulls you down from a 3:**
- Explaining diagrams in textbook language without connecting to the article. If your explanation works for any article on the same topic, it is generic.
- Including only one diagram when the article clearly warrants more than one.
- Including diagrams the article does not support (e.g. a subsidy diagram when the article does not discuss subsidies).
- Claims in the explanation that have no basis in the article (e.g. asserting excess supply with no evidence).

**1/3: What to do at minimum:**
- Include a relevant diagram.

**What pulls you down from a 2:**
- Missing explanations, or explanations that are incorrect.
- Missing critical labels that the explanation refers to (e.g. explaining a tariff but not labelling it on the diagram).
- Muddled or error-filled explanations (e.g. confusing MSB and MSC).

**0/3:** Diagrams are irrelevant to the article, or the explanations contradict the diagrams.

## Criterion B: Terminology (2 marks)

> This criterion does not mention definitions. You do not need to define terms. A paragraph of definitions wastes your word count and earns nothing.

**2/2: What to do:**
- Use relevant economic terms throughout your commentary (not just the opening paragraph) and use them correctly.
- Show you understand the terms by applying them naturally in your analysis, in relation to the article.

**1/2: What to do at minimum:**
- Include some relevant economic terms in the commentary.

**What pulls you down from a 2:**
- Using incorrect terms (e.g. "devaluation" when you mean "depreciation," or "overproduction" when the issue is overconsumption).
- Front-loading all your terminology into a definitions paragraph instead of using terms naturally throughout.
- Using acronyms without ever writing the full term, especially acronyms not in the IB syllabus.

**0/2:** No relevant economic terminology is used, or terms are entirely incorrect. This is rare.

## Criterion C: Application and Analysis (3 marks)

> This is where most students lose marks they could have earned. The difference between 2 and 3 is not about knowing more economics. It is about connecting your economics to the article.

**3/3: What to do:**
- Apply relevant economic theory throughout your commentary.
- Use specific data, figures, quotes, and facts from the article to drive your analysis. Your analysis should not make sense without the article.
- When you explain a shift, a surplus, a welfare loss, or any other economic outcome, tie it to something the article actually says.

**2/3: What to do at minimum:**
- Apply relevant theory throughout the commentary.
- Provide analysis that is theoretically correct.

**What pulls you down from a 3:**
- Analysis that could be copy-pasted into a commentary about a different article on the same topic. If it still works, it is not contextualised enough for a 3.
- Paragraphs that read like a textbook essay on the theory without referencing article specifics.
- Using external sources. The IA is not a research assignment; all analysis must come from your selected article.
- Using "will" for outcomes that are uncertain, rather than "may" or "could."

**1/3: What to do at minimum:**
- Apply some relevant theory to the article.

**What pulls you down from a 2:**
- Analysis that is mostly descriptive, restating what the article says rather than applying theory to it.
- Significant errors in the economic theory applied.
- Entire paragraphs that have no connection to the article.

**0/3:** No relevant economic theory is applied.

## Criterion D: Key Concept (3 marks)

> The nine key concepts: scarcity, choice, efficiency, equity, economic well-being, sustainability, change, interdependence, intervention. You must use one of these. Each concept can only be used once across your three commentaries.

**3/3: What to do:**
- Choose a key concept that genuinely fits the article.
- Refer to the concept at multiple points throughout the commentary, not just at the start and end.
- Each time you use the concept, explain how it connects to something specific in the article. Show you understand the concept in its economic sense.

**2/3: What to do at minimum:**
- Identify a valid key concept.
- Explain the link between the concept and the article in at least some parts of the commentary.

**What pulls you down from a 3:**
- The link is not sustained throughout. You connect the concept in some places but drop it in others.
- The explanation relies on assumptions rather than article content.
- Using the concept as a label (e.g. bolding the word "intervention" repeatedly) without explaining why the specific policy in the article is an example of it.

**1/3: What to do at minimum:**
- Identify a valid key concept and make some attempt to connect it to the article.

**What pulls you down from a 2:**
- The link is vague or barely developed.
- The concept is named but not meaningfully connected to the article.

**0/3:** The concept is not one of the nine, it only appears on the coversheet, it is explained incorrectly (e.g. "sustainability" in a non-economic sense), or it has already been used in another commentary.

---

## Criterion E: Evaluation (3 marks)

> Evaluation is not a conclusion that summarises your analysis. It is a judgment: is this policy effective? For whom? Under what conditions? What are the trade-offs?

**3/3: What to do:**
- Make judgments about significance, effectiveness, or trade-offs, and support every judgment with evidence from the article.
- Consider multiple perspectives. "Balanced" does not mean listing equal pros and cons. It means showing you have thought about the issue from more than one angle (e.g. short-run vs. long-run, different stakeholders, conditions under which the outcome changes).
- Synthesise in your conclusion: pull together your evaluation into a reasoned overall judgment, not a summary of what you already said.

**2/3: What to do at minimum:**
- Make judgments supported by some reasoning.

**What pulls you down from a 3:**
- Reasoning that is theoretical with no connection to the article (e.g. discussing PED without connecting it to the specific good in the article).
- Evaluation that is one-sided rather than balanced.
- Unsupported personal opinions ("I think the government should...").
- Policy suggestions or alternatives not mentioned or supported by the article.
- Restating the article author's own evaluation rather than developing your own.

**1/3: What to do at minimum:**
- Make some attempt at evaluation.

**What pulls you down from a 2:**
- Judgments are mostly unsupported.
- The evaluation reads like a summary or conclusion rather than a judgment.
- Alternatives or opinions offered with no basis in the article.
- Using external sources or research to build evaluation.

**0/3:** No attempt at evaluation, or the evaluation does not reach the standard described above.
