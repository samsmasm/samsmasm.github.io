# 1. University of Auckland — BSc Mathematics

**Catalogue:** https://study.auckland.ac.nz / https://courseoutline.auckland.ac.nz

## Programme Structure

**Total:** 360 points. Each course = 15 points.

**Year 1 (Stage I — 100-level):**
- MATHS 120 Algebra *(core)*
- MATHS 130 Calculus *(core)*
- MATHS 162 Computational Mathematics *(corequisite with 120)*
- MATHS 108 General Mathematics 1 *(alternative entry for students not yet ready for 120/130)*

**Year 2 (Stage II — 200-level):**
- MATHS 250 Algebra and Calculus 2 *(core)*
- MATHS 253 Algebra and Calculus 3 *(core)*
- MATHS 254 Fundamental Concepts of Mathematics *(core)*
- MATHS 260 Differential Equations *(core)*
- MATHS 270 Numerical Computation *(core for Applied pathway)*

**Year 3 (Stage III — 300-level):**

*Pure Mathematics pathway:*
- MATHS 320 Algebraic Structures
- MATHS 332 Real Analysis
- MATHS 333 Analysis in Higher Dimensions
- MATHS 315 Mathematical Logic

*Applied Mathematics pathway:*
- MATHS 340 Real and Complex Calculus
- MATHS 361 Partial Differential Equations
- MATHS 362 Methods in Applied Mathematics

*All pathways:*
- MATHS 399 Capstone: Mathematics *(recommended final year)*

**Entry:** At least 18 credits in NCEA Level 3 Mathematics with at least 9 at Merit/Excellence. Equivalents: B in CIE A2 Maths; 5/7 in IB Mathematics AA (SL or HL).

**Fees (2025):** Not published centrally; UoA domestic tuition ~NZ$7,500–8,000/year estimate.

**Minimum pass rule:** All courses require at least 35% in the final exam to pass the course overall.

## Year 1 Courses

### MATHS 108 — General Mathematics 1
**Points:** 15 | **Level:** Stage I

**Description:** Entry-level course for commerce and social sciences students with Year 13 Maths. Linear functions and equations, matrices, limits, differential calculus of one and two variables, integral calculus of one variable.

**Learning Outcomes:**
1. Display mastery of the algebra concepts covered.
2. Solve problems involving functions and/or calculus.
3. Apply mathematical techniques to real-life problems.

**Prerequisites:** MATHS 102 or at least 13 credits in NCEA Level 3 Mathematics. Weaker entry than MATHS 120/130.

**Assessment:** Tutorials, quizzes, assignments, final exam (breakdown published on Canvas per semester).

**Textbook:** Custom coursebook (SRC/Canvas).

---

### MATHS 120 — Algebra
**Points:** 15 | **Level:** Stage I

**Description:** Linear algebra and introduction to mathematical language and reasoning. Complex numbers, induction, combinatorics, systems of linear equations, vector and matrix operations.

**Learning Outcomes:**
1. Understand and write logical mathematical arguments.
2. Read and use mathematical language and notation fluently.
3. Work confidently with complex number algebra.
4. Solve systems of linear equations of several variables.
5. Perform a wide variety of vector and matrix operations algebraically and geometrically.

**Prerequisites:** 18 credits in NCEA Level 3 Maths with at least 9 at Merit/Excellence; or B in CIE A2 Maths; or 5/7 in IB Maths AA SL/HL. Corequisite: MATHS 162.

**Assessment:** Must achieve at least 35% in the exam and 50% overall to pass.

**Textbook:** MATHS 120 Algebra Coursebook 2025 (custom, not a commercial textbook).

---

### MATHS 130 — Calculus
**Points:** 15 | **Level:** Stage I

**Description:** Rigorous single-variable calculus alongside introduction to mathematical thinking. Sets, functions, limits (rigorous definition), differentiation and integration (techniques and underlying theorems).

**Learning Outcomes:**
1. Understand and write logical mathematical arguments.
2. Read and use mathematical language and notation.
3. Use the language of sets and functions to describe mathematical objects.
4. Calculate limits rigorously.
5. Differentiate and integrate functions and understand the theorems behind those techniques.
6. Use differentiation and integration to optimise and study behaviour of a wide variety of objects.

**Prerequisites:** Same as MATHS 120. Workload: 3 hrs lectures + 1 hr tutorial + ~6 hrs self-study per week.

**Textbook:** MATHS 130 Calculus Coursebook 2025 (custom). Khan Academy tutorial videos linked through Canvas.

## Year 2 Courses

### MATHS 250 — Algebra and Calculus 2
**Points:** 15 | **Level:** Stage II

**Description:** Sequel to MATHS 120 and 130. Multivariable calculus and linear algebra — real number completeness, sequences and power series, basics of multivariable calculus, linear algebra including dynamical systems and graph theory.

**Learning Outcomes:**
1. Critically analyse logical arguments and use advanced techniques in algebra and calculus to solve problems.
2. Identify and apply linear algebra techniques to problems in further mathematics, the sciences and real-world settings.
3. Demonstrate command of fundamentals underpinning calculus (completeness, sequences, series, multivariable calculus).
4. Identify and apply calculus techniques across further mathematics and the sciences.
5. Apply the connection between linear algebra and geometry in contexts including dynamical systems and graph theory.
6. Express logical mathematical arguments with sufficient detail, precision and structure.
7. Gain experience in group work and communicating mathematical ideas to others.

**Prerequisites:** MATHS 120 and 130 (or ENGGEN 150 / ENGSCI 111 / equivalent).

**Assessment:** Mid-semester test (Week 7, evening). Minimum 35% in exam to pass. Full breakdown on Canvas.

**Textbook:** Lecture manuals (linear algebra and calculus) available electronically and printed through the SRC. MATHS 250 Algebra and Calculus 2 Coursebook Sem 1 2025 (ubiq bookshop).

---

### MATHS 253 — Algebra and Calculus 3
**Points:** 15 | **Level:** Stage II

**Description:** Sequel to MATHS 250. Jordan normal form, diagonalisation of Hermitian operators, Fourier approximations, quadratic forms. Multivariable calculus: partial derivatives, gradient, optimisation, double/triple/line integrals, conservative vector fields, Green's theorem.

**Learning Outcomes:**
1. Display a high level of knowledge of linear algebra and calculus.
2. Demonstrate understanding of the mutual penetration and inseparability of algebra and calculus.
3. Solve problems requiring knowledge of both algebraic and calculus ideas.
4. Work in teams to critically discuss approaches to complex applied problems.
5. Engage in critical interaction about the role of mathematics in society.

**Prerequisites:** MATHS 250.

**Assessment:** Minimum 35% in exam required to pass.

**Textbook:** Study guide/course notes provided by the department.

---

### MATHS 254 — Fundamental Concepts of Mathematics
**Points:** 15 | **Level:** Stage II

**Description:** Foundations not covered in the core courses: equivalence relations, number theory, counting, elementary probability, geometry, symmetry, and metric spaces. Emphasis on mathematical rigour and beauty.

**Topics:** Relations, partitions, orderings; Euclidean division, prime factorisation, modular arithmetic; metric spaces (open/closed/bounded sets, sequences); symmetry and introduction to groups; combinatorics and probability (pigeonhole principle, elementary probability).

**Learning Outcomes:**
1. Develop and apply set theory, equivalence relations, and number theory.
2. Understand metric spaces: convergence, continuity, compactness.
3. Use combinatorial and probabilistic techniques.
4. Apply elements of group theory to geometry and symmetry.
5. Engage in rigorous proof and precise mathematical expression.

**Prerequisites:** MATHS 250 (corequisite or prerequisite).

**Assessment:** Two compulsory tests (first and second half of course). Minimum 35% in final exam to pass.

**Textbook:** None — detailed course notes provided on Canvas (definitions, examples, proofs, exercises, background references).

---

### MATHS 260 — Differential Equations
**Points:** 15 | **Level:** Stage II

**Description:** Methods for understanding ODE behaviour. Qualitative, elementary numerical, and analytical methods. Applications to scientific modelling.

**Learning Outcomes:**
1. Identify analytic, qualitative, and numerical methods suitable for ODE analysis.
2. Use simple analytic, qualitative, and numerical methods as appropriate.
3. Assess, describe, and modify simple ODE models of physical phenomena.
4. Write appropriately detailed and structured mathematical arguments in simple contexts.

**Prerequisites:** MATHS 208 or MATHS 250 (or concurrent enrolment in 250).

**Textbook:**
- *Differential Equations* — Blanchard, Devaney, Hall. Any edition; lecturers use the 4th ed. Copies on Short Loan. Also: custom printed coursebook (~$20).

---

### MATHS 270 — Numerical Computation
**Points:** 15 | **Level:** Stage II | Core for Applied pathway

**Description:** Computer-based methods for approximating solutions to problems in science and engineering. Linear and nonlinear algebraic equations, interpolation, differentiation, integration, numerical ODE solving. Uses MATLAB.

**Learning Outcomes:** Prepares students for Stage III Applied Mathematics courses requiring computational techniques.

**Prerequisites:** MATHS 120 + 130 (or equivalent 15 pts). Also requires prior computing course (COMPSCI 101/105/130, INFOSYS 110/120, or MATHS 162/199).

**Textbook:**
- *Guide to Scientific Computing* — Peter Turner. CRC Press, 2nd ed. (2000).
- Software: MATLAB (instruction included in course).

## Year 3 Courses

### MATHS 315 — Mathematical Logic
**Points:** 15 | **Level:** Stage III

**Description:** First-order predicate logic, model theory, and the relationship between models and mathematical structures.

**Prerequisites:** B+ or higher in COMPSCI 225, MATHS 254, MATHS 255, or PHIL 222.

**Assessment:** Assignments 20% + Test 20% + Exam 60%; or 100% exam (whichever is higher). Minimum 35% in exam.

---

### MATHS 320 — Algebraic Structures
**Points:** 15 | **Level:** Stage III

**Description:** Groups, rings, and fields. Abstract descriptions with concrete examples. Applications to symmetries, geometry, coding theory, and cryptography.

**Learning Outcomes:**
1. Display basic knowledge of groups, rings, and fields and their properties.
2. Analyse and answer questions through examples and methods.
3. Understand connections with other disciplines and real-world applications.
4. Demonstrate understanding of the nature of proof and present arguments formally and informally.

**Prerequisites:** MATHS 250 and MATHS 254 (or 255).

**Assessment:** Minimum 35% in final exam to pass. Workload: ~36 hrs lectures, 11 hrs tutorials, 36 hrs reading, 67 hrs assignment prep per semester.

**Textbook:**
- *Contemporary Abstract Algebra* — J. A. Gallian, 8th ed. or later. Also detailed course notes.

---

### MATHS 332 — Real Analysis
**Points:** 15 | **Level:** Stage III

**Description:** Foundational mathematics underlying calculus. Properties of real numbers (completeness, Archimedean property), sequences and series, limits, continuous functions, differentiation, Riemann integration, sequences and series of functions, uniform convergence.

**Learning Outcomes:**
1. Develop and apply theory of series and convergence tests.
2. Develop and apply theory of continuous functions and classical theorems.
3. Develop and apply theory of differentiable functions and classical theorems.
4. Develop and apply theory of Riemann integrals and classical theorems.
5. Develop and apply theory of uniform convergence.

**Prerequisites:** MATHS 250, and MATHS 254/255 or A or higher in MATHS 253 and 260.

**Assessment:** Assignments 20% + Test 20% + Exam 60%; or 100% exam. Minimum 35% in exam.

---

### MATHS 333 — Analysis in Higher Dimensions
**Points:** 15 | **Level:** Stage III

**Description:** Derivative in higher-dimensional settings. Analysis in R^n, Inverse Function Theorem, Implicit Function Theorem, Banach Contraction Theorem.

**Prerequisites:** MATHS 332, or B or higher in MATHS 254.

**Assessment:** Assignments 30% + Test 20% + Exam 50%; or 100% exam. Minimum 35% in exam.

---

### MATHS 340 — Real and Complex Calculus
**Points:** 15 | **Level:** Stage III | Core for Applied pathway

**Description:** Multivariate and complex calculus. Time derivatives in non-Cartesian coordinates, higher-dimensional integrals, Green's/Stokes'/Divergence theorems, complex calculus and the Residue Theorem.

**Learning Outcomes:**
1. Calculate time derivatives in non-Cartesian coordinates.
2. Calculate double, triple, surface, volume, and line integrals.
3. Use Green's and Stokes' theorems and the divergence theorem.
4. Evaluate complex and improper integrals using the Residue Theorem.

**Prerequisites:** MATHS 250. Recommended: MATHS 253.

**Textbook:**
- *Mathematical Methods for Engineers and Scientists* (Vols 1 & 2) — K.-T. Tang, Springer. Free via UoA library online.

---

### MATHS 361 — Partial Differential Equations
**Points:** 15 | **Level:** Stage III | Core for Applied pathway

**Description:** Fourier series, Fourier integrals, boundary value problems, separation of variables, Laplace and Fourier transform solutions, distributions and Green's functions. Application to second-order PDEs in 1, 2, and 3 dimensions.

**Learning Outcomes (partial):**
- Set up boundary conditions for Laplace, diffusion, and wave equations in 2 and 3 dimensions.
- Use Green's functions to solve simple ODEs.

**Prerequisites:** MATHS 260.

---

### MATHS 362 — Methods in Applied Mathematics
**Points:** 15 | **Level:** Stage III | Core for Applied pathway

**Description:** Three applied topics: (1) First-order PDEs and traffic flow modelling (method of characteristics, asymptotic analysis); (2) Parameter estimation for mathematical models; (3) Asymptotic analysis. Uses MATLAB.

**Prerequisites:** MATHS 260 and MATHS 270. Recommended: MATHS 253, MATHS 361.

---

### MATHS 399 — Capstone: Mathematics
**Points:** 15 | **Level:** Stage III

**Description:** Mathematics in modern society and as a human discipline. Exploration of the role of mathematics in society and culture; activities of mathematicians as teachers, critics, and innovators.

**Learning Outcomes:** Skills in communication, critical thinking, teaching, and creative problem solving.

**Prerequisites:** MATHS 250 and 30 points at Stage III in Mathematics.

# 2. University of Warwick — BSc / MMath Mathematics

**Module list:** https://warwick.ac.uk/fac/sci/maths/currentstudents/modules/
**Undergraduate handbook:** https://warwick.ac.uk/fac/sci/maths/currentstudents/ughandbook/

## Programme Structure

**BSc Mathematics:** 3 years. No compulsory core modules — entirely optional from the module list. At least 50% of Year 3 credit must be from Mathematics modules.

**MMath Mathematics (G103):** 4 years.
- Year 1: 8 core modules (~75% of year's workload)
- Year 2: 5 core modules + essay (~55% of year's workload)
- Year 3: No core; at least 50% must be maths
- Year 4: Specialist and graduate-level modules

**Credit system:** CATS (Creditability and Achievement Transfer Scheme). Typical module = 10–15 CATS. Full year = 120 CATS.

**Fees (2025/26):** Domestic (UK) ~£9,250/year; International rates vary by programme.

---

## Year 1 Modules

### MA131 — Analysis
**CATS:** 24 | **Year:** 1 (MMath core; popular BSc choice)

**Description:** First excursion into pure mathematics for first-year undergraduates. Sequences and series (Term 1), continuity and differentiability (Term 2), Riemann integration (Term 1 of Year 2). Based on the work of Cauchy, Dirichlet, Weierstrass, Bolzano, D'Alembert, and Riemann. Develops mathematical rigour and the ability to form one's own methods for problems.

**Learning Outcomes (from module page):**
- What do we mean by 'infinity'?
- How can you accurately compute the value of π, e, or √2?
- How can you add up infinitely many numbers, or infinitely many functions?
- Can all functions be approximated by polynomials?

By the end: construct epsilon-delta proofs, prove standard theorems about sequences, series, continuous and differentiable functions, and Riemann integrals.

**Assessment:** Weekly assignments (15%) + January exam (25%) + June exam (60%).

**Teaching:** 1 lecture/week + 2 support classes/week (1 hr each). Emphasis on self-directed problem-solving beyond rote technique application.

---

### MA132 — Foundations
**CATS:** 10 | **Year:** 1

**Description:** Bridges school and university mathematics. Begins with concrete calculation techniques and progresses toward abstraction and proof.

**Topics:**
- Logic: variables, booleans, negation, operations, quantifiers, tautologies, deduction rules
- Proof: direct, contraposition, contradiction, construction, cases; induction, pigeonhole principle, counting
- Complex numbers: polar and exponential form, De Moivre's Theorem, nth roots
- Set theory: operations (∩, ∪), Venn diagrams, de Morgan's Laws, Cartesian products, power sets

**Learning Outcomes:** Work with number systems and their properties; use mathematical language of sets and quantifiers, functions and relations; become familiar with proof methods.

**Assessment:** Module partially examined at start of Term 2. Students do not need to pass all components to pass overall.

---

### MA106 — Linear Algebra
**CATS:** 12 | **Year:** 1

**Topics:**
- Linear maps and isomorphism of vector spaces
- Matrices and algebraic operations
- Matrix reduction (row and column operations)
- Applications to solving linear equations; rank
- Eigenvalues and eigenvectors and geometric significance
- Diagonalising matrices with distinct eigenvalues

**Assessment:** Weekly assignments (15%) + 2-hour exam in June (85%).

**Contact:** 3 lectures per week.

---

### MA133 — Differential Equations
**CATS:** 10 | **Year:** 1

**Description:** Cornerstone of applied mathematics. First-order equations, linear second-order equations, coupled first-order linear systems with constant coefficients, div/grad/curl operations, integration over simple curves and surfaces.

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Solve first-order, linear second-order, and coupled systems of first-order ODEs.
- Manipulate div, grad, and curl operations.
- Integrate over simple curves and surfaces.
- Acquire key reasoning and problem-solving skills.

**Assessment:** Marked assignments + exam. No requirement to pass all components separately.

---

### MA134 — Geometry and Motion
**CATS:** 12 | **Year:** 1 | **Term:** Term 2

**Description:** Connection between geometry and motion via curves in space. Integration of vector-valued functions and functions of two and three variables: surface area, volume, circulation of a fluid around a closed curve. Combines geometry (area, volume) with motion (circulation).

**Assessment:** Weekly assignments (15%) + 2-hour exam in June (85%).

---

## Year 2 Modules (selected)

### MA222 — Metric Spaces
**CATS:** 10 | **Year:** 2

**Description:** Normed spaces, metric spaces, and topological spaces. Fundamental properties of compactness, connectedness, and completeness.

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Understand basic concepts, theorems, and calculations of normed, metric, and topological spaces.
- Understand topological spaces as a generalisation of metric spaces; understand metrisability.
- Understand compactness and its relation to continuity; understand connectedness.
- Understand Cauchy sequences and completeness; understand completion of non-complete spaces.

**Prerequisite:** Year 1 Analysis courses. Prerequisite for MA3F1 Introduction to Topology.

---

### MA241 — Combinatorics
**CATS:** 10 | **Year:** 2 (also offered as MA341 at Year 3 level)

**Description:** Finite mathematical structures. Counting problems and graph theory.

**Topics — Enumerative Combinatorics:** Basic counting (with/without repetition), binomial coefficients and Binomial Theorem, multinomial theorem, inclusion-exclusion, linear recurrence relations and Fibonacci numbers, generating functions and Catalan numbers, permutations, partitions, Stirling and Bell numbers.

**Topics — Graph Theory:** Basic concepts, trees, planarity, matching theory, elements of Ramsey theory, Boolean functions.

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Combine covered techniques with creativity to solve combinatorial problems.
- Adapt ideas to understand new combinatorial structures.
- Adapt proof ideas to new settings.

---

### MA251 — Algebra I: Advanced Linear Algebra
**CATS:** 12 | **Year:** 2

**Description:** Continuation of Year 1 Linear Algebra. Extends the study and points toward applications in other branches of mathematics and physics.

**Topics:**
- Jordan canonical form of matrices and linear maps
- Bilinear forms and quadratic forms; canonical bases
- Smith normal form for matrices over the integers; finitely generated abelian groups

**Learning Outcomes:** Familiarity with theory and computation of Jordan canonical form; bilinear and quadratic forms; Smith normal form and its application to finitely generated abelian groups.

**Prerequisites:** MA106 Linear Algebra and MA132 Foundations.

---

### MA252 — Combinatorial Optimisation
**CATS:** 10 | **Year:** 2

**Description:** Introduction to combinatorial optimisation. Fundamental graph theory problems and algorithms: shortest paths, minimum spanning trees, matchings, network flows.

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Apply combinatorial structures (graphs, matroids) and algorithmic techniques (BFS, DFS) to describe and solve fundamental problems (shortest path, travelling salesman).

---

### MA259 — Multivariable Calculus
**CATS:** 10 | **Year:** 2

**Description:** Extends Year 1 analysis to the multivariable context.

**Topics:** Continuous vector-valued functions, differentiable functions, Inverse and Implicit Function Theorems, vector fields, maxima/minima/saddles, Green's Theorem, Divergence Theorem.

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Demonstrate understanding of basic concepts, theorems, and calculations of multivariate analysis.
- Understand the Implicit and Inverse Function Theorems and their applications.
- Understand vector fields, Green's Theorem, and the Divergence Theorem.
- Classify critical points using Taylor expansions.

---

### MA267 — Groups and Rings
**CATS:** 10 | **Year:** 2

**Description:** Introductory abstract algebra. Groups and rings as abstract algebraic structures, with examples and applications. (Replacement/supplement for the older MA249.)

---

### MA250 — Partial Differential Equations
**CATS:** 10 | **Year:** 2

**Description:** Introduction to the basic phenomenology of PDEs and their solutions.

# 3. Australian National University (ANU) — BSc Mathematics

**Course catalogue:** https://programsandcourses.anu.edu.au/major/math-maj

## Programme Structure

**Total:** 48 units. Each course = 6 units.

**Year 1 — Two pathways:**

*Standard pathway:*
- MATH1013 Mathematics and Applications 1
- MATH1014 Mathematics and Applications 2

*Advanced pathway (for students with high mathematical proficiency):*
- MATH1115 Advanced Mathematics and Applications 1
- MATH1116 Advanced Mathematics and Applications 2

Optional preparation (does not count toward major): MATH1003

**Years 2–3 (2000- and 3000-level):**
- Minimum 36 units from MATH 2000/3000-level courses
  - Maximum 18 units from 2000-level
  - Minimum 18 units from 3000-level
- Up to 12 units from allied disciplines (ASTR, COMP, PHYS, STAT at 2000/3000-level)
- Students aiming for Honours should take the advanced pathway (MATH1115/1116)

**Fees (2026):** Domestic AUD$4,920/course × 8 courses ≈ AUD$39,360 total. International: AUD$7,020/course.

---

## Year 1 Courses

### MATH1013 — Mathematics and Applications 1
**Units:** 6 | **Level:** Year 1 (standard pathway)

**Description:** Single-variable calculus and introductory linear algebra. Practical application across physical sciences, engineering, IT, economics, and commerce. Limits, continuity, differentiation, transcendental functions, integration, linear systems, matrix algebra, determinants, complex numbers.

**Learning Outcomes:**
1. Explain fundamental concepts of calculus and linear algebra and their role in modern mathematics.
2. Demonstrate proficiency in calculus and linear algebra techniques.
3. Show mathematical reasoning through concept explanation and proof reproduction.
4. Apply problem-solving across diverse science/engineering contexts.

**Prerequisites:** ACT Specialist Mathematics Major-Minor or NSW HSC Mathematics Extension 1 (recommended). Students with lower preparation should complete MATH1003 first. Cannot enrol if already completed MATH1113 or MATH1115.

**Assessment:**
- Workshop contributions: 7%
- Online quizzes: 7%
- Two assignments: 18%
- Mastery hurdle examinations: 20%
- Final examination: 48%

**Textbooks:**
- *Linear Algebra* (4th ed. or later) — David Lay
- *Essential Calculus* (2nd ed.) — James Stewart

---

### MATH1014 — Mathematics and Applications 2
**Units:** 6 | **Level:** Year 1 (standard pathway)

**Description:** Builds on MATH1013. Integration techniques, sequences and series, multivariable functions, vector spaces, eigenvalues, and orthogonality.

**Learning Outcomes:**
1. Explain calculus and linear algebra concepts including vector spaces, eigenvalues, integration, and multivariable functions.
2. Demonstrate proficient use of calculus and linear algebra techniques.
3. Show mathematical reasoning through concept explanation and proof reproduction.
4. Apply problem-solving to physics, engineering, and mathematical contexts.

**Prerequisites:** MATH1013, MATH1115, or MATH1113. Cannot enrol if MATH1116 previously completed.

**Assessment:**
- Workshop assessments: 8%
- Online quizzes: 12%

- Final examination: 50%

**Textbooks:**
- *Linear Algebra* (3rd ed. or later) — David Lay
- *Essential Calculus* (2nd ed.) — James Stewart

---

### MATH1115 — Advanced Mathematics and Applications 1
**Units:** 6 | **Level:** Year 1 (advanced pathway)

**Description:** In-depth study of calculus and linear algebra with emphasis on mathematical foundations and proofs. Calculus/analysis: suprema, infima, integration, limits, continuity, differentiation, Taylor polynomials. Linear algebra: linear equations, vector spaces, determinants, eigenvalues, complex numbers.

**Learning Outcomes:**
1. Explain fundamental concepts of calculus and linear algebra in modern and applied contexts.
2. Demonstrate accurate and efficient use of calculus and linear algebra techniques.
3. Show mathematical reasoning capacity through analysis, proof, and concept explanation.
4. Apply problem-solving across physics, engineering, and other fields.

**Prerequisites:** Satisfactory pass in ACT Specialist Mathematics double major or NSW HSC Mathematics Extension 2. Students with excellent results in Extension 1 may enrol with advisor approval.

- Weekly assignments and online quizzes: 30%
- In-class tests (two): 25%
- Final examination: 45%

**Textbooks:**
- *Elementary Linear Algebra: Applications Version* — Anton and Rorres
- *Calculus* — Michael Spivak

### MATH1116 — Advanced Mathematics and Applications 2
**Units:** 6 | **Level:** Year 1 (advanced pathway)

Calculus/analysis: sequences, series, convergence, power series, Taylor series, metric spaces, multivariable limits and continuity, double integrals. **Assessment:**
- Assignments and online quizzes: 25–30%

- Final examination: 35–45%

**Textbook:**
- *Linear Algebra Done Right* (3rd ed.) — Sheldon Axler
- Analysis course notes via LMS

**Textbooks:**
- *A First Course in Differential Equations with Modelling Applications* — Dennis G. Zill
- *Essential Calculus* — James Stewart

---

### MATH2306 — Applied Mathematics II

**Assessment:**

# 4. MIT — BSc Mathematics (Course 18)

**Programme overview:** https://math.mit.edu/academics/undergrad/

## Programme Structure

**Duration:** 4 years (standard US undergraduate). Units system: L-R-P (lecture-recitation-preparation hrs/week). Standard course = 12 units. Full load ≈ 48 units/year.

**Four degree options:**

| Option | Key Requirement |
|--------|----------------|
| General (Course 18) | 18.03 or 18.032 + Linear Algebra + 8 additional 12-unit subjects (≥6 advanced, ≥3 distinct areas) |
| Applied (Course 18) | Above plus 18.04 or 18.112, 18.200, 18.300; electives distributed across applied groups |
| Pure (Course 18) | 18.100 Real Analysis is basic; 18.701 Algebra I after proof experience |
| Mathematics with Computer Science (18C) | Combined degree, separate requirements |

**Communication Intensive (CI-M):** At least 2 subjects must be designated CI-M to fulfil MIT's communication requirement.

**Entry:** No specific pre-entry requirements for MIT overall (selective undergraduate admissions). Mathematics-specific placement via advanced standing exams or AP/IB credits.

## Core Undergraduate Courses

### 18.01 — Single Variable Calculus
**Units:** 5-0-7 (12 total) | **Level:** U | **Offered:** Fall, Spring

**Description:** Differentiation and integration of functions of one variable, with applications. Relates small-scale (differential) quantities to large-scale (integrated) quantities via the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

**Topics:** Differentiation rules; graphing and optimisation; indefinite and definite integration; integration techniques; polar coordinates; L'Hopital's rule; improper integrals; infinite series.

**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand the integral as a cumulative sum, the derivative as a rate of change, and the inverse relationship between them.
- Use both the limit definition and rules of differentiation to differentiate functions.
- Sketch graphs using asymptotes, critical points, and derivative tests for increasing/decreasing and concavity.

**Prerequisites:** None (high school algebra and trigonometry assumed). Credit by advanced standing exam or AP/IB transfer.

**Course structure:** 3 lectures/week (1 hr each) + 2 recitations/week (1 hr each).

**Assessment (representative):**
- 8 problem sets (due Fridays; one may be turned in late)
- 4 in-class exams (50 min each)
- 1 final exam (3 hours)

**Textbook:** Prof. Gilbert Strang's calculus textbook (Wellesley-Cambridge Press). Free OCW open textbook: *RES.18-001 Calculus Open Textbook* (Fall 2023) also available.

**OCW:** https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/18-01-single-variable-calculus-fall-2006/

### 18.02 — Multivariable Calculus
**Units:** 5-0-7 (12 total) | **Level:** U | **Offered:** Fall, Spring (required of all MIT undergraduates)

**Description:** Calculus of several variables. Vector algebra in 3-space, determinants, matrices. Vector-valued functions of one variable, space motion. Scalar functions of several variables: partial differentiation, gradient, optimisation. Double and line integrals in the plane; exact differentials and conservative fields; Green's Theorem. Triple integrals, line and surface integrals in space; Divergence Theorem; Stokes' Theorem.

**Prerequisites:** 18.01 (Single Variable Calculus) — GIR (General Institute Requirement).

**Course structure:** Lectures + recitations. 12 problem sets.

**Assessment (Fall 2007):**
Total 900 points:
- Problem sets (12 sets): 250 pts
- Four in-class 50-minute exams: 400 pts (100 pts each)
- Final exam: 250 pts

**Textbooks:**
- Edwards, H.C. and Penney, D.E. *Multivariable Calculus*, 6th ed. Prentice Hall, 2002. ISBN: 9780130339676.
- *18.02 Supplementary Notes and Problems* — Prof. Arthur Mattuck (distributed in course; available on OCW).

**OCW:** https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/18-02-multivariable-calculus-fall-2007/

### 18.03 — Differential Equations
**Units:** 12 total | **Level:** U | **Offered:** Fall, Spring

**Description:** First-order ODEs (analytical, graphical, numerical). Linear ODEs with constant coefficients. Complex numbers and exponentials. Inhomogeneous equations (polynomial, sinusoidal, exponential inputs). Oscillations, damping, resonance. Fourier series. Matrices; linear systems of ODEs; phase plane. Matrix exponential. Nonlinear autonomous systems; energy and limit cycles. Brief introduction to PDEs. Applications from mechanical and electrical engineering.

**Learning Objectives (nine core skills):**
1. Model a simple system to obtain a first-order ODE.
2. Visualise solutions using direction fields and isoclines; approximate using Euler's method.
3. Solve a first-order linear ODE by integrating factors or variation of parameters.
4. Calculate with complex numbers and exponentials.
5. Solve a constant-coefficient second-order linear IVP with driving term (exponential × polynomial).
6. Compute amplitude gain and phase shift for sinusoidal input signals.
7. Compute Fourier coefficients; find periodic solutions of linear ODEs using Fourier series.
8. Use the Laplace transform and its inverse; apply to solve IVPs.
9. Analyse nonlinear autonomous systems using the phase plane.

**Prerequisites:** 18.01. Corequisite: 18.02 (may be taken simultaneously).

**Assessment (Spring 2010):**
Total ~885 points. Approximately one-third each to:
- Homework (problem sets)
- Three one-hour in-class exams
- Three-hour comprehensive final examination

**Textbooks:**
- Edwards, C. and Penney, D. *Elementary Differential Equations with Boundary Value Problems*, 6th ed. Prentice Hall, 2003. ISBN: 9780136006138. (5th ed. ISBN 9780131457744 also acceptable.)
- Supplementary notes: *18.03 Notes and Exercises* — Arthur Mattuck; *18.03 Supplementary Notes* — Haynes Miller.

**OCW:** https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/18-03-differential-equations-spring-2010/

### 18.06 — Linear Algebra
**Units:** 3-0-9 (12 total) | **Level:** U | **Offered:** Fall, Spring

**Description:** Matrix theory and linear algebra for use in other disciplines (physics, economics, social sciences, engineering). Systems of equations, vector spaces, determinants, eigenvalues, similarity, and positive definite matrices. Goals: "using matrices and also understanding them."

**Topics (full list):**
Geometry of linear equations; elimination; multiplication and inverse matrices; LU factorisation; transposes, permutations, vector spaces; column space and nullspace; solving Ax = 0 and Ax = b; independence, basis, dimension; four fundamental subspaces; graphs and networks; orthogonal vectors and subspaces; projections; projection matrices and least squares; Gram-Schmidt; determinant properties, formulas, cofactors; Cramer's Rule, inverse, volume; eigenvalues and eigenvectors; diagonalisation and powers of A; differential equations and exp(At); Markov matrices; Fourier series; symmetric matrices and positive definiteness; complex matrices; FFT; Jordan form; SVD; linear transformations; change of basis; image compression; pseudoinverse.

**Prerequisites:** 18.02 (Multivariable Calculus).

**Tools:** Some problems use MATLAB (no prior experience required).

**Assessment (Spring 2010):**
- Problem sets: 15%
- Three one-hour exams: 45% (15% each)
- Final exam: 40%
- No calculators or notes in exams.

**Textbook:**
- Strang, G. *Introduction to Linear Algebra*, 4th ed. Wellesley-Cambridge Press, 2009. ISBN: 9780980232714.

**OCW:** https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/18-06-linear-algebra-spring-2010/

### 18.100 — Real Analysis (variants: A, B, C, P, Q)
**Units:** 12 (18.100C: 15) | **Level:** U | **Offered:** Fall, Spring

**Variants:**
- **18.100A** — Gentler introduction, one-variable analysis, de-emphasises point-set topology. For students with no prior proof experience. Recitation format.
- **18.100B** — Standard, more demanding. Includes metric spaces and point-set topology.
- **18.100C** — 15 units. As 18.100B plus further instruction in written and oral communication. Fulfils MIT CI-M (Communication Intensive) requirement.

**Official Description:** Fundamentals of mathematical analysis: convergence of sequences and series, continuity, differentiability, Riemann integral, sequences and series of functions, uniformity, interchange of limit operations. Teaches understanding and construction of proofs through the study of real numbers.

**Topics:**
- Sets, set operations, mathematical induction
- Real numbers, completeness axiom, Archimedean property
- Sequences and series: convergence, absolute convergence
- Continuity on the real line (18.100A) / on metric spaces (18.100B)
- Differentiability; Mean Value Theorem
- Riemann integral
- Sequences and series of functions; uniform convergence
- Point-set topology / metric spaces (18.100B)

**Prerequisites:** 18.02 (GIR). Some students benefit from 18.090 (Intro to Mathematical Reasoning) or 18.06 first. 18.100A is for students without prior proof experience.

**Assessment:**

*18.100A (Fall 2020):*
- Assignments 1–12: 50%
- Midterm exam: 25%
- Final assignment: 25%

*18.100B (Fall 2010):*
- 10 homework assignments (lowest 2 dropped): weight
- Four in-class 30-minute quizzes: weight
- 3-hour final exam: weight
- Grade computed two ways; better used:
  - Method 1: Homework 30% + Quizzes 30% + Final 40%
  - Method 2: Final exam only

**Textbooks:**

*18.100A:* Lebl, J. *Basic Analysis I: Introduction to Real Analysis, Vol. 1.* 2018. ISBN: 9781718862401. (Free PDF download available.)

*18.100B:* Rudin, W. *Principles of Mathematical Analysis*, 3rd ed. McGraw-Hill, 1976. (Other texts: Apostol *Mathematical Analysis*, 2nd ed.; Spivak *Calculus*, 4th ed.)

**OCW:** https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/18-100a-real-analysis-fall-2020/ / https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/18-100b-analysis-i-fall-2010/

### 18.701 — Algebra I
**Units:** 3-0-9 (12 total) | **Level:** U | **Offered:** Fall

**Description:** Groups, vector spaces, linear transformations, symmetry groups, bilinear forms, and linear groups. More extensive and theoretical than the 18.700–18.703 sequence.

**Topics:** Definitions, subgroups, homomorphisms, cosets, quotient groups; group actions; Sylow theorems; symmetry groups; rings and ring homomorphisms; vector spaces; linear transformations; bilinear forms; linear groups (GL, SL, O, U).

**Prerequisites:** 18.100A, 18.100B, 18.100P, or 18.100Q (Real Analysis), or 18.090 (Intro to Mathematical Reasoning), or permission of instructor. Prior proof experience essential.

**Course Format:** Students prepare by completing assigned readings and exercises before class. ~10–11 weekly problem sets. Diagnostic problem at start of term.

**Assessment:** Problem sets are the most important component. Exams included (exact breakdown on OCW syllabus page).

**Textbook:**
- Artin, M. *Algebra*, 2nd ed. Addison Wesley, 2010. ISBN: 9780132413770.

**OCW:** https://ocw.mit.edu/courses/18-701-algebra-i-fall-2010/

### 18.702 — Algebra II
**Units:** 3-0-9 (12 total) | **Level:** U | **Offered:** Spring

**Description:** Sequel to 18.701. Group representations, rings, ideals, fields, polynomial rings, modules, factorisation, integers in quadratic number fields, field extensions, Galois theory.

**Topics:** Regular representation; rings and homomorphisms; factoring integer polynomials; prime ideals; modules; ruler and compass constructions; symmetric functions and discriminant; main theorem of Galois theory; group representations.

**Prerequisites:** 18.701 (Algebra I).

- Weekly problem sets (~8–9): 25%
- Three quizzes: 75% (25% each)

**Textbook:**

# 5. University of Melbourne — BSc Mathematics and Statistics (supplementary)

**Handbook:** https://handbook.unimelb.edu.au/2026/

> Note: Melbourne's Mathematics and Statistics major requires 50 credit points with four specialisation pathways. Individual subject pages have partial public detail (LOs available; assessment and textbooks vary). Three subjects sampled below.

---

### MAST10012 — Introduction to Mathematics
**Credit Points:** 12.5 | **Level:** Undergraduate level 1 | **Semester:** 1

**Description:** Strengthens algebraic and conceptual skills as preparation for calculus. Number systems and set theory; counting principles; probability theory; functions (logarithmic, exponential, trigonometric); differential and integral calculus with practical applications.

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Master fundamental concepts of number systems, counting techniques, logic, and set notation.
- Comprehend mathematical functions, domains, ranges, and inverse functions.
- Apply transformations and operations for graphing function types.
- Use differentiation rules for optimisation and curve sketching.
- Perform integration techniques; calculate areas under and between curves.
- Calculate probabilities for discrete and continuous random variables.

**Generic Skills:** Problem-solving, analytical reasoning, teamwork, time management.

---

### MAST10021 — Calculus 2: Advanced
**Credit Points:** 12.5 | **Level:** Undergraduate level 1 | **Semester:** 2

**Description:** Advanced calculus mirroring MAST10006 (Calculus 2) but with greater depth, rigour, and emphasis on proofs. Multivariable functions, limits, continuity, partial derivatives, chain rules, extrema. Reviews single-variable differentiation and integration; integration techniques (parts, substitution). ODEs (first and second order). Hyperbolic functions, Taylor polynomials, sequences, series, complex functions and power series.

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Evaluate limits for single and multivariable functions.
- Apply continuity theorems to analyse function properties.
- Determine sequence and series convergence/divergence.
- Work with hyperbolic and inverse hyperbolic functions.
- Execute integration using parts, substitution, and partial fractions.
- Solve first and second-order ODEs for modelling physical systems.
- Compute Taylor polynomials.
- Calculate partial derivatives and gradients for optimisation.
- Construct simple mathematical proofs.

---

### MAST20026 — Real Analysis
**Credit Points:** 12.5 | **Level:** Undergraduate level 2 | **Semesters:** 1 and 2

**Description:** Careful theoretical framework and applications for mathematical analysis. Rigorous proofs; distinction of real from rational numbers; sequences and series convergence; continuous and differentiable functions (Mean Value Theorem, Taylor's theorem); Riemann integration; power series; Fourier series for periodic functions.

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Construct rigorous proofs using contradiction, contraposition, and induction.
- Apply field and order axioms to real number facts.
- Use epsilon-delta proofs for theoretical and approximation purposes.
- Evaluate sequence convergence properties.
- Apply continuous and differentiable function theory.
- Interpret Riemann integrals as limits of sums.
- Determine convergence of infinite series and improper integrals.
- Apply power series expansions and Taylor polynomial approximations.
- Explain Fourier series' role in representing periodic functions.
