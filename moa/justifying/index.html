Justifying Sources

Part A of the assessment requires you to write 6 mini-paragraphs justifying each of your sources. You do this using the OPCVL framework: explaining how the origin, purpose and/or content of each source affect its value and limitations for your investigation.

From the assessment (Part A): "In 6 mini paragraphs, explain why you chose this source, with reference to the value and limitations of each source. Do this using the OPCVL framework. That is, explain how the origin, purpose and/or content affect the value and limitations of the source."

A good place to start is the Sutori story on OPCVL and source analysis:

Sutori: Source Analysis and OPCVL ↗

The OPCVL Framework

O
Origin
  • Who created this source?
  • When and where was it created?
  • What type of source is it?
P
Purpose
  • Why was this source created?
  • Who was the intended audience?
  • What was the creator trying to achieve?
C
Content
  • What does the source say or show?
  • What information is relevant to your RQ?
  • What perspective does it represent?
V
Value
  • How does its origin, purpose, or content make it useful?
  • Why is this source well-suited to your investigation?
L
Limitations
  • How does its origin, purpose, or content create weaknesses?
  • What might this source leave out, exaggerate, or distort?
How to Structure Each Mini-Paragraph
1
Identify the source. Name it and state briefly what it is (origin).
2
Explain its value. Refer to its origin, purpose, or content to explain why it is useful for your investigation.
3
Explain its limitations. Refer to its origin, purpose, or content to explain what it cannot tell you, or where it may be biased or incomplete.

Each paragraph is around 50 words. You are not writing a summary of the source; you are evaluating it as a tool for your investigation.

Key distinction: Value and Limitations must connect back to origin, purpose, or content. Don't just say a source is "useful" or "biased" - explain why, with reference to what it is and how it was made.

Step 3: Write your 6 paragraphs

One mini-paragraph per source (around 50 words each). For each paragraph:

  • Identify the source (origin)
  • Explain one or two values, linked to origin, purpose, or content
  • Explain one or two limitations, linked to origin, purpose, or content
  • Italicise any non-English words
Bring your paragraphs to class one at a time - your teacher will give feedback on individual paragraphs so you can refine your approach before writing all six.
