What is the History IA?

This internal assessment (IA) draws on the International Baccalaureate History IA. The skills you will develop in this assessment will help you in many areas in future school and university courses. These include systematically developing critical thinking skills such as analysis, synthesis and evaluation. It's also an opportunity to acquire deep knowledge and understanding of a given topic.

What does the History IA consist of?

It consists of 3 sections:

  • Criterion A: Identification and evaluation of sources
  • Criterion B: Investigation
  • Criterion C: Reflection
What are my (Student) responsibilities?

To be successful you need to have ownership of both the process and results of the investigation, so you must be proactive in both attitude and conduct. This is an opportunity to engage in material you are genuinely interested in or research a unique topic that is not often explored - potentially a type of question we have not looked at in Foundations of the Modern World.

Academic honesty is very important, as is ensuring you follow the 10-year rule! According to the IB, '... the topic must be historical, and therefore cannot be on an event that has happened in the last 10 years.

What sources should I use? Where can I find them?

Ms. Wong will guide you through the SSIS Library databases. You will draw all search journals, primary sources, and books. You will need to base your entire IA on:

  • Two scholarly journal articles, or chapters from a scholarly book.
  • Two reference articles, e.g. from Brittanica
  • Two primary source materials.
When are my deadlines for the History IA?

See the Checkpoints in the presentation and on the calendar.

Top tips
  • Chunk it - bring me paragraphs, introductions etc.
  • Carefully select your sources
  • Don't miss deadlines. There will be consequences
  • Consider the difference between an interpretation and a source
  • Carefully consider WHERE you are getting your information

Questions and Sources(Parts 1, 2, and Final Product) Can list types of sources but does not understand how they may be helpful in answering questions. Identifies sources that could be helpful but lacks consideration of perspectives or potential uses. Chooses sources that are helpful in answering questions considering multiple perspectives and uses. Strategically determines sources, deeply analyzing their perspectives, types, and potential uses in a nuanced way.
Definitions The student lists source types in the bibliography but does not understand or explain how the two selected sources in Part A are helpful in answering the research question. The student identifies an appropriate research question and two sources, but the explanation of their relevance in Part A lacks consideration of historical perspectives or specific potential uses. The student clearly states an appropriate research question and chooses two relevant sources for Part A, considering how their different perspectives help answer the question. The student strategically determines sources for the bibliography (including primary academic and artifacts) and provides a nuanced analysis of their perspectives and specific relevance to the inquiry in Part A.
Time, Continuity, and Change(Parts 1, 3, and Final Product) Identifies key events in history but struggles to connect them in a meaningful narrative. Recognizes patterns and themes of historical continuity and change; understands basic cause and effect. Analyzes in depth the causes of change and continuity over time; makes connections between different historical periods. Synthesizes a comprehensive understanding of history, recognizing nuanced patterns and developing original interpretations of historical processes.
Definitions The background information in Part B identifies key historical events but struggles to connect them into a meaningful narrative that supports the thesis. The introduction and body paragraphs recognize basic patterns of cause and effect and themes of historical change related to the research question. The investigation analyzes the causes of change in depth; Part C reflects on how chronology and themes were used to make connections between historical periods. The student synthesizes a comprehensive understanding of the topic, using Part C to develop original interpretations of historical processes and the challenges of methodology.
Using Evidence(Part 2 and Final Product) Gathers information from limited sources with minimal regard to their origins or authority. Gathers information from a range of sources but inconsistently evaluates their credibility. Systematically gathers and evaluates information from a wide range of sources effectively. Critically gathers and evaluates information, demonstrating an exceptional ability to discern source quality and relevance.
Definitions The student gathers information from limited sources and provides a Part A evaluation that ignores the authority or origins of the evidence. The student uses the required range of sources (academic and artifacts) but provides an inconsistent evaluation of their "Value" and "Limitation" in Part A. The student systematically evaluates the Origin, Purpose, and Content (OPCV) of sources effectively in Part A and uses strategic short quotations in Part B. The student demonstrates an exceptional ability to discern source quality in Part A and critically integrates evidence from primary artifacts throughout the investigation.
Communicating Conclusions(Final Product) Makes simple claims without clear evidence; overlooks counterclaims and evidentiary weaknesses. Makes claims with some evidence; acknowledges counterclaims and evidentiary weaknesses to some extent. Constructs arguments with precise claims and relevant evidence; recognizes counterclaims and evidentiary weaknesses. Constructs nuanced arguments with insightful claims; integrates evidence from multiple sources; thoroughly addresses counterclaims and evidentiary weaknesses.
Definitions The student makes simple claims in Part B without clear evidence and overlooks counterclaims or weaknesses in the evidence provided. The student provides a thesis and supporting evidence but only acknowledges different historical perspectives (historiography) or counterclaims to a limited extent. The student constructs a precise thesis and uses relevant evidence to support arguments, while clearly recognizing counterclaims and different historical interpretations. The student constructs a nuanced argument with insightful claims, thoroughly addressing historiographical conflicts and synthesizing evidence into a comprehensive final conclusion.
