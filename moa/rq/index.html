Your Research Question

After selecting your event from 1990–2015, you need to ensure you are building a Research Question, not just a descriptive report. While they might look similar, they lead to very different projects.

A Description

"The Berlin Wall fell in 1990, and people celebrated."

An Inquiry

"I wonder... if the fall of the wall actually improved the economy for the average person in East Berlin immediately, or if it made life harder?"

The Rule of Thumb

TypeAnswers
REPORT Who, What, Where, and When?
RQ How, Why, and To what extent?

The Two Smart People Test

"Can two smart people look at the same facts and come to two different conclusions for this question?"
If YES →You have a Research Question. You are ready to build an argument.
If NO  →You have a Report. You've just found a more detailed set of facts, but nothing to prove.

Supporting Questions: The Building Blocks

Once you have your Research Question, you need a plan to answer it. You can't answer a big "How" or "Why" without gathering the facts first. These are your Supporting Questions.

Think of it this way:

  • The RQ is the destination of your journey.
  • The SQs are the turn-by-turn directions to get there.

The Comparison

Research Question (The Big Idea) Supporting Questions (The Evidence)
What is it? A provocative question with no single right answer. Specific questions that focus on facts, definitions, and data.
What does it do? It sparks a debate and forces you to make an argument. They provide the raw info you need to back up your claims.
How does it start? To what extent... or Was it inevitable that... Who... What... Where... or How did [X] work?

An Example

The RQ (The Argument):

"I wonder... to what extent did the 2008 Financial Crisis cause the rise of the Occupy Wall Street movement?"

The SQs (The Facts you need to find):

  • What were the specific causes of the 2008 housing market collapse?
  • Who were the original organizers of the Occupy Wall Street protests?
  • How many cities worldwide saw protests inspired by the movement in 2011?

The Balance Test

RQ only →You have an opinion but no proof.
SQs only →You have a pile of facts but no point.

To succeed as an historian, you need one big RQ supported by three or four rock-solid SQs.

The "Historian vs. Reporter" Challenge

Both categories require research. However, a Report (R) asks you to describe how things were, while a Research Question (RQ) asks you to argue why things changed or to what extent an event mattered.

Drag each question card into either the REPORT or RESEARCH QUESTION box. When you've placed them all, click Check Answers.
