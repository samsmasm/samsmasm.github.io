How to Cut Your Word Count

1
Do a quote audit
Go through your essay and highlight every direct quote. For each one, ask: does the exact wording matter here, or am I just using this because it was easier than paraphrasing? If it's the latter, rewrite it in your own words and replace the attribution with a bracket citation. This is usually the fastest way to cut words from a draft that's over the limit.
2
Cut or merge your weakest paragraph
Read through your body paragraphs and find the one doing the least work, either repeating a point you've already made or making a point that isn't central to your argument. Cut it entirely, or strip out the one useful sentence and fold it into an adjacent paragraph.
3
Reduce your examples
If any paragraph has three or more examples, cut to two. You can acknowledge the broader pattern in one sentence: "This dynamic is visible across multiple contexts (Smith, 2021; Jones, 2022)." You keep the analytical point and lose the repetition.
4
Do a filler word pass
Once you've made the big structural cuts, do a final read looking for "the", "that", "in order to", "due to the fact that", and similar padding. Read each sentence without the suspect word. If nothing is lost, delete it. This won't save as much as the structural cuts but it cleans up the writing.
5
Repeat
Go back to Step 1 and go again. Each pass through the essay will find cuts you missed the first time. Most students need at least two full cycles before the word count is genuinely under control.
